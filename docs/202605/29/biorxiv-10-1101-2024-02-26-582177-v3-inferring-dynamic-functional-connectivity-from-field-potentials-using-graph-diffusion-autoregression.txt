Estimating dynamic functional connectivity (dFC) is attracting increased attention, 5

spurred by rapid advancements in multi-site neural recording technologies and e ff orts 6

to better understand cognitive processes. Yet, most studies focus on static estimates 7

of functional connectivity that cannot capture highly dynamic neural processes, while 8

also ignoring information about the structural organization of the brain. To address 9

these issues, we introduce a class of network-constrained linear autoregressive models 10 

that give rise to a highly dynamic functional connectivity signal on the edges of a 11 

predefined structural connectivity graph. Furthermore, we demonstrate that adding 12 

an additional diffusion constraint improves the model’s performance. We successfully 13 

validated the resulting graph diffusion autoregressive (GDAR) model on simulated 14 

neural activity and recordings from subdural and intracortical micro-electrode arrays 15 

placed in macaque sensorimotor cortex demonstrating its ability to describe rapid 16 

communication dynamics induced by optogenetic stimulation, changes in resting state 17 

dFC following stroke and electrical stimulation, and neural correlates of behavior 18 

during a reach task. 19

Introduction 21 

The coordinated interactions across different brain networks and subnetworks underlies cognitive 22 

processes 1–6 , and disruptions of these interactions are linked to a range of neurological disorders 7– 23 10 . Despite this demonstrated importance, we still do not fully understand how brain networks 24 

perform computations through the coordinated signaling of connected neurons and neural 25 

populations during natural behavior, following a disease or injury, or as the result of rehabilitative 26 

intervention. The development of new electrophysiological recording technologies such as large-27 

scale micro-electrode arrays provides unique opportunities for addressing this gap by measuring 28 

brain network activity simultaneously over multiple areas with high spatial and temporal 29 

resolution 11–16 .30 

A common signal extracted from subdural and intracortical micro-electrode arrays is the local 31 

field potential (LFP) , which describes voltage fluctuations in the extracellular space of neuronal 32 

tissue. For these signals, the most common approach for estimating neural communication is 33 

through functional connectivity (FC) analysis 17,18 . In general, FC measures define neural 34 

communication as the undirected (symmetric) or directed (asymmetric) statistical dependence 35 

between different measurements that can be inferred from data using either model-free approaches 36 

or very general model classes such as vector autoregressive (VAR) models 19–22 . While these 37 

techniques are a popular choice for electrophysiology analysis, they predominantly yield static 38 

estimates of neural communication. Additionally, they rarely incorporate information about the 39

To address these limitations, we propose a new approach for estimating dynamic functional 42 

connectivity (dFC) that 1) naturally incorporates the spatial layout of the recording array and 43 

the local connectivity structure of the cortex 23 as a structural prior and 2 ) produces a highly 44 

dynamic and directed connectivity signal that can be used to study transient network events. Our 45 

approach is based on a class of network-constrained linear autoregressive models that can be fit 46 

to neural data. We consider two specific models of this class: 1) a standard vector autoregressive 47 

model whose parameters are constrained to a predefined structural connectivity graph called graph 48 

autoregressive (GAR) model and 2) a Laplacian autoregressive model, where network interactions 49 

are modeled via the graph Laplacian. Because the graph Laplacian is commonly used to model 50 

diffusion processes on networks 24 , we refer to the latter approach as the graph diffusion 51 

autoregressive (GDAR) model . Furthermore, we introduce new post processing techniques for 52 

analyzing the graph-spectral properties of network-constrained directed FC estimates that are 53 

based on the Hodge decomposition 25–28 .54 

To demonstrate the utility of our framework, we tested the GAR and GDAR model on five highly 55 

diverse datasets. First, using synthetic data from various networks of Wilson-Cowan oscillators 56 

we demonstrate that the high-resolution dFC signal estimated by the GDAR model better 57 

captures the simulated communication signal than GAR and standard VAR model s. Furthermore, 58 

we show that GAR and GDAR model s better generalize to unseen data than VAR models. Finally, 59 

using three micro-electrocorticography ( 𝜇𝜇 ECoG) and one Utah array dataset we demonstrate that 60 

the GDAR model can be used to uncover transient communication dynamics evoked during 61 

cortical optogenetic stimulation, characterize changes in resting state FC after stroke and electrical 62 

stimulation, and uncover network-level dFC correlates of a monkey’s reach behavior that are 63 

dependent on the spatial frequency. W e show that the GDAR model outperforms alternative 64 

approaches and provides insights that cannot be obtained by other models. 65

Graph autoregressive (GAR) and graph diffusion autoregressive (GDAR) model. 67 

GAR and GDAR models are linear vector autoregressive (VAR) models, whose parameters are 68 

constrained to the connections of a predefined structural connectivity graph. A n overview of both 69 

models is shown in  Fig. 1  and a more detailed mathematical description can be found in Methods. 70 

First, the electrode layout of the recording array is used to construct a sparse and locally connected 71 

graph, with each electrode representing a node and with edges connecting nearby nodes  Fig. 1 a72 

left ). This graph serves as a structural prior that incorporates information about the local 73 

connectivity of the cortex into the model 23 . The models then transform the neural activity into 74 

directed FC or flow signals defined on the graph edges ( Fig. 1 a right). Th ese signals, which we will 75 

refer to as 

GAR flow and 

GDAR flow , respectively, describe the predictive information flow 76 

between the nodes. Unlike classical FC analysis, which aggregates information over multiple time 77 

points, thereby estimating an average information flow , the GAR and GDAR models transform 78 

the neural activity at each time point into a flow signal without losing temporal resolution .79 

Therefore, they can be used to study highly transient communication events in the brain. 80

The GDAR model outperforms VAR and GAR models in inferring communication 129 

dynamics in a network of coupled Wilson-Cowan oscillators: To assess the accuracy of 130 

the GAR/GDAR flow, we use simulated data generated by 10 randomly connected 16-node 131 

networks of coupled Wilson-Cowan oscillators (Fig. 2  and  Extended Fig. 2 a). The networks are 132 

used to generate a ground truth neural flow signal, as well as simulated neural activity, which is 133 

used to fit GAR, GDAR , and VAR models of varying model order s (see Methods). All models are 134 

used to transform the simulated neural activity into a neural flow signal, which is compared to the 135 

ground truth neural flow using various metrics (Fig. 2a). Furthermore, we have estimated 136 

traditional FC metrics via coherence or metrics from the Granger causality (GC) family. GC can 137 

be computed from the spectral representation of VAR model parameters leading to two metrics 138 

that are popular in neuroscience: partial directed coherence (PDC) 22 and directed transfer function 139 

(DTF) 21 . For all VAR -based metrics (VAR flow, PDC, DTF) , which assume fully connected model 140 

matrices, the resulting FC estimate is only compared over edges that are present in the structural 141 

connectivity networks. 142 

First, we assessed the accuracy of the estimate flow signal using spectral analysis for a single model 143 

order ( 𝑝𝑝 = 24 ). In  Fig. 2 b, we compared the spectral magnitudes of coherence, PDC, DTF, and 144 

estimated flow with the ground truth flow spectral magnitude using Peason correlation coefficients. 145 

We found that the GDAR flow yields significantly higher correlations than any other metric ( Fig. 146 

2d; Wilcoxon rank-sum test, 𝑝𝑝 < 0.05 ). Since GDAR and GAR are based on autoregressive models, 147 

we can compute equivalent PDC and DTF metrics from their model parameters (see Methods). 148 

While GDAR -PDC/DTF outperform GAR - and traditional PDC/DTF they do not outperform 149 

any of the flow metrics ( Extended Fig. 2 d). Furthermore, w e found that the GDAR model exhibits 150 

consistently lower magnitude errors than the VAR and G AR models for all frequencies and lower 151 

phase errors for frequencies below 50 Hz ( Fig. 2 c). These findings also generalize to other model 152 

orders. Specifically, for all 𝑝𝑝 ≥ 4, correlations with the ground truth magnitude spectra were 153 

highest for the GDAR model ( Fig. 2 d) suggesting that it captures neural interactions more 154 

accurately than the alternative metrics. We found that the same observation also holds when 155 

computing correlation between estimated and ground truth flows in the time domain ( Extended 156 

Fig. 2 f). 157 

A notable observation is that the spectral magnitude of the ground truth flow is well approximated 158 

by lower order VAR, GAR, and GDAR models. Specifically, the median correlation between 159 

estimated and ground truth spectral magnitude for VAR and GAR models decreases with 160 

increasing model order. In contrast, the correlation for the GDAR model reaches another local 161 

maximum at higher orders, where it significantly outperforms the other two models ( Fig. 2 d). 162 

Despite this second maximum, the highest median correlations for the GDAR model still occur at 163 

low model orders. At the same time, because low-order models rely on fewer past time steps for 164 

predicting future activity, they may have limited capacity to capture more complex spatiotemporal 165 

dynamics that are not fully reflected in spectral magnitude alone. To test this , we used a recently 166 

developed tool from dynamical systems theory, called dynamical similarity analysis (DSA) 33 , which 167 

uses dynamical mode decomposition and shape analysis to provide a dissimilarity score between 168 

two (high -dimensional) time series . We found that with increasing model order the accuracy in 169 

capturing the spatiotemporal dynamics improves for all models (decreased DSA dissimilarity 170 

score) before plateauing at an order around 𝑝𝑝 = 24 (Fig. 2 e). Hence, to accurately capture the 171

Application of GDAR model to electrophysiological recordings: Due to better 190 

performance of the GDA R over the GAR model on simulated data, we will focus on the GDAR 191 

model for the remainder of the paper. To show the versatility of the GDAR model for analyzing 192 

network-level dFC evoked by cortical stimulation, during behavior, and at rest, we have applied 193 

the model to electrophysiological recordings from four separate experiments that either use μ ECoG 194 

arrays (3 datasets) or a Utah array (1 dataset) to record LFPs from the sensorimotor cortex of 195 

macaque monkeys . For all datasets, the layout of the recording array was first used to construct 196 

a locally connected and sparse graph—with nodes corresponding to the recording channels— 197 

mimicking the local connectivity structure of the cortex 23 . Next, GDAR models of different orders 198 

were fit to the recorded LFPs. The resulting model coefficients were then used to transform the 199 

LFPs into GDAR flow signals, which were post -processed depending on the experimental 200 

paradigm. 201 

The GDAR model uncovers communication dynamics evoked by cortical o ptogenetic stimulation: 202 

First, we show that the GDAR model can uncover fast, stimulation induced communication 203 

dynamics that match the experimental protocol. To do so we used three sessions from an 204 

optogenetic stimulation experiment performed in macaques, where two lasers repeatedly 205 

stimulated different locations of the primary motor (M1) and somatosensory (S1) cortex expressing 206 

the opsin C1V1, and fit a 5 th order GDAR model to the LFPs recorded by a 96 -channel 𝜇𝜇 ECoG 207 

array during stimulation (see  Fig. 3 a and b and Methods) 11,34,35 . GDAR flow signals averaged over 208 

all stimulation trials in the milliseconds before and after stimulation for Session 1 are shown in 209 

Fig. 3 c and Supplementary Video 1. When the network is at rest, flow levels across the network 210 

are small. Activation by the first laser located in M1 causes the GDAR flow to immediately 211 

increase near the stimulation location before spreading further into the network and reaching S1. 212 

After the second laser was activated, the flow increases near the second stimulation location and 213 

spreads into most parts of the network within the next few milliseconds. 214 

It is apparent from  Fig. 3 c that the GDAR flow exhibits complex spatiotemporal dynamics within 215 

milliseconds after stimulation. To test how these dynamics depend on the stimulation pattern, we 216 

project the high-dimensional flow signal from three sessions , which only differ in their stimulation 217 

location (see  Fig. 3 a), onto their first two principal components (PCs) and compare the flow 218 

dynamics in this lower dimensional subspace (see Methods). We found that these low dimensional 219 

GDAR flow dynamics are very consistent within each session and strongly differ between sessions 220 

(Fig. 3 d). Furthermore, the flow dynamics show some remarkable similarities with the stimulation 221

Discussion 338 

By drawing insights from computational neuroscience, statistical modeling, and signal processing 339 

over networks, we have introduced the GAR and GDAR model as members of a class of graphically 340 

constrained autoregressive models for estimating network-level dFC from field potential recordings. 341 

In contrast to classical VAR models that are a popular tool for electrophysiological FC analysis, 342 

our approach incorporates the spatial layout of the recording array as a structural prior, thereby 343 

significantly reduc ing the model complexity while mimicking cortical connectivity on a344 

macroscopic scale. Furthermore, we demonstrate how our models give rise to a flow signal with 345 

the same temporal resolution as the original recordings, making them well suited for analyzing 346 

both transient and long-term FC patterns. Finally, network communication in the GDAR model 347 

is driven by gradients of neural activity—rather than activity itself as is the case for GAR and 348 

VAR model s—resulting in a diffusion -like process. Using simulations and four electrophysiology 349

A drawback of standard VAR models is that they ignore spatial relations between the recording 354 

electrodes, which means that interactions between nearby sensors are treated equally to 355 

interactions between distant ones. However, especially for cortical recordings it is likely that signal 356 

propagation is dominated by short range connections due to the local connectivity structure of 357 

the cortex found in mice and macaque monkeys 23 . The idea of integrating spatial information in 358 

the form of structural priors into standard VAR models and other FC measures has recently been 359 

proposed in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), electroencephalography (EEG), and 360 

magnetoencephalography (MEG) studies, where it has been shown to improve the estimation of 361 

FC networks 48–51 . However, to the best of our knowledge, it has not been applied to localized 362 

recording arrays that focus on network dynamics within one or two cortices. By adding structurally 363 

informed priors, the GAR and GDAR model have approximately five to ten times fewer parameters 364 

than the full VAR model for the electrode arrays used in our analysis resulting in less overfitting 365 

to idiosyncrasies in the data. The problem of overfitting is particularly evident in the poor 366 

generalization performance of the VAR model in simulations and for the reach dataset where 367 

model fitting relies on a limited number of observations (see  Fig. 2 f and  Extended Fig. 2 h). 368 

Modeling dFC on top of a network also has the advantage that we can perform graph spectral 369 

analysis on the resulting dFC signal. This allows us to decompose complicated network-wide 370 

communication patterns into simpler and more interpretable modes representing feedforward 371 

(gradient) and feedback (curl ) signal flow dynamics. Similar node -centric graph spectral 372 

decomposition techniques have recently been proposed to analyze neural data 52–57 , however these 373 

have exclusively focus on node signals rather than FC networks. 374 

An important finding of our study is that the GDAR model outperforms the GAR model on 375 

simulated data even though the GAR model uses the same structural prior and is more expressive. 376 

Therefore, the better performance of the GDAR model must be attributed to the additional 377 

diffusion constraint incorporated into the model. Indeed, diffusive processes have previously been 378 

proposed as possible a mechanism for neural communication 29,30 . It has also been shown that 379 

diffusion processes can explain FC estimates 58 and model the propagation of activity evoked by 380 

intracranial stimulation more accurately than alternative models of neural communication 59 .381 

Furthermore, the Laplacian that drives the diffusion process in the GDAR model has been used 382 

in neural field models to simulate realistic large -scale brain dynamics 60,61 . Therefore, on a383 

macroscopic level, the diffusion constraint may add a biophysically realistic mechanism to the 384 

model that aids in its overall performance. Nevertheless, we want to emphasize that the GDAR 385 

model is still a phenomenological model which is in contrast with dynamic causal modeling where 386 

the goal is to provide insights into biological mechanisms of neural communication 62–65 .387 

Using activity gradients as a driver of neural communication also has additional practical 388 

advantages for processing field potential signals . Such signals often suffer from spurious 389 

correlations due to volume conduction, signal artifacts that are shared across channels, and the 390 

common reference signal problem. These spurious correlations are known to cause erroneous 391

Another key difference between existing dFC frameworks and our approach is that we propose a 399 

new mechanism for obtaining temporally resolved connectivity estimates. Existing approaches 400 

typically derive such dynamics through sliding windows 68–70 or adaptive parameter estimation 51,71 .401 

In contrast, we achieve this by combining static model parameter with the recorded neural activity 402 

resulting in a dynamic FC signal with maximal temporal resolution . This approach offers several 403 

advantages: it reduces the number of parameters that need to be estimated, and it enables the 404 

detection of short, transient communication events that may be smoothed out by sliding window 405 

approaches or are difficult to track using linear adaptive parameter estimation techniques. Using 406 

simulations, we also demonstrated that combining parameters with neural activity produces more 407 

accurate FC estimates than purely parameter-based techniques such as PDC and DTF (Fig. 2 b408 

and  Extended Fig. 2 d) .409 

Our approach has several limitations. The assumption of a locally connected nearest neighbor 410 

graph as a structural prior inspired by the local connectivity structure of the cortex neglects the 411 

potential existence of any direct long-range propagation paths. Since it can be difficult to 412 

determine the best underlying network structure as precise structural information is often not 413 

available, we suggest that in the future the structural connectivity graph could be designed in a 414 

more data-driven way, for example, using sparsity and distance regularizers. Furthermore, we 415 

currently make no distinction between nodes corresponding to electrodes in the interior versus the 416 

boundary of the array. Especially the boundary nodes may exhibit a large exchange of information 417 

with regions outside the array, which is not captured by the model, but could be incorporated by 418 

adding additional input terms. Another promising avenue would be to explore how other proposed 419 

mechanisms of neural signaling, such as biased random walks or shortest path routing 29,30 , could 420 

be incorporated as constraints into data-driven dFC models. Furthermore, the GAR and GDAR 421 

model only capture linear interactions, but could be extended to model non-linear communication 422 

dynamics by introducing activation functions into the model. 423 

Although we developed and tested our new framework using field potential recordings , we believe 424 

it can be extended to other neural recording modalities and applications. For instance, proposed 425 

models may be adapted for spiking data, either by modifying the autoregressive component to 426 

accommodate for discrete point processes—such as through generalized linear models 72 —or by 427 

first converting spikes into firing rate s. The models should also be readily applicable to human 428 

ECoG and stereoelectroencephalography (sEEG) recordings, which share similar signal properties 429 

with the recordings analyzed here. Finally, GAR and GDAR models can be applied to brain-wide 430 

recoding modalities such as EEG , MEG or functional MRI (fMRI), which —combined with 431 

estimated structural connectivity networks—can enable the analysis of large-scale brain dynamics. 432

Decomposition into gradient and curl flow spectra: Similar to the classical Fourier 541 

transform for time series, where a signal can be decomposed into a series of oscillatory components 542 

of increasing frequency, a flow signal can be decomposed into a set of spatial components (flow 543 

signals) with increasing spatial frequency. Furthermore, a flow signal can be decomposed into 544 

gradient ( non-circular ) components, which have non -zero divergence (sum of in -flow minus out-545 

flow) for some or all nodes of the graph, and curl components, which have zero divergence for all 546 

graph nodes. This can be achieved via the Hodge-decomposition that defines two orthogonal sets 547 

of spatial basis functions (defined on the edge domain) for a given graph 25–27 . A directional graph 548 

flow signal, such as the GDAR flow at each timestep 𝑡𝑡 , can then be projected onto these sets of 549 

basis functions to obtain the gradient and curl flow spectrum. Note that this decomposition 550 

assumes a unidirectional flow 𝑓𝑓 {𝑖𝑖 ,𝑗𝑗 } for each edge in the graph, where the sign of 𝑓𝑓 {𝑖𝑖 ,𝑗𝑗 } together 551 

with the edge reference direction defined in 𝐁𝐁 determines the flow direction. For bidirectional flow 552 

signals 𝑓𝑓 {𝑖𝑖→𝑗𝑗 } ≠ 𝑓𝑓 {𝑗𝑗→𝑖𝑖 } such as for the GAR or VAR models, we first need to obtain a unidirectional 553

GDAR model can also be adapted to model longer signal propagation paths between specific 1212 

nodes in the network. For example, this may be the case for connections across the sulcus that 1213 

separate M1 and S1. To model this, we have constrained the GDAR model to enforce a 1214 

minimum propagation delay of 4 ms for all edges that connect nodes in M1 with nodes in S1 1215 

(see Methods). The evolution of the GDAR flow for Session 1 averaged over all trials is shown in 1216 

Supplementary Video 2 and is similar to the dynamics observed in  Fig. 3 d and Supplementary 1217 

Video 1, but with noticeably less flow across the sulcus. The PC reduced flow dynamics across 1218 

all sessions are shown in  Extended Fig. 3 f and are almost identical to the dynamics shown in 1219 

Fig. 3 e, suggesting that adding signal propagation constraints to the model does not negatively 1220 

impact the sensitivity of the GDAR flow signal to the stimulation parameters. 1221
