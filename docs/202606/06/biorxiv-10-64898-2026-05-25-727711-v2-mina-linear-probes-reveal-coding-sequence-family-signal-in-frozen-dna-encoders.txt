## Abstract 

Motivation: Frozen DNA encoders are often used as genomic feature extractors, but downstream fine-tuning does not show what information is already linearly accessible in their unchanged embeddings. We introduce MINA (Model Interrogation of Nucleotide Architectures), a lightweight probing benchmark for testing whether frozen DNA embeddings can recover (i) a 5-way protein-family label for each gene and (ii) the 1,536-dimensional GenePT embedding of each gene’s natural-language summary. We compare recoverability between canonical coding sequence and TSS-centred genomic contexts. 

Results: In 3,244 human protein-coding genes from five families, frozen encoders recovered the family-annotation target most clearly from coding sequence. NT-v2 with meanD pooling reached macro-F1 0.828 / κ 0.821, compared with 0.672 / κ 0.702 for a CDS 4-mer baseline. Alignment to GenePT natural-language descriptions was weaker. Replacing CDS with 196,608 bp TSS-centred windows substantially reduced performance across all four encoders, indicating that the recoverable signal is primarily coding-sequence family signal rather than generic gene-function signal from arbitrary genomic context. 

Availability and implementation: Source code: https://github.com/Austin-Senna/dna_to_text; Python ≥3.11. 

Keywords: DNA language models; nucleotide transformers; linear probing; coding sequence; GenePT; protein-family classification; genomic representation learning.

## 1 Introduction 

Pretrained DNA encoders such as DNABERT-2 (Zhou et al. , 2024), Nucleotide Transformer (Dalla-Torre et al. , 2025), GENA-LM (Fishman 

et al. , 2025), and HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al. , 2023) are increasingly used as genomic feature extractors. They differ in tokenisation, architecture, context length, and pretraining corpus, yet their embeddings are often treated as interchangeable summaries of biological sequence. Strong downstream performance after fine-tuning, however, does not show what information is already present in the frozen representation. Linear probing (Alain and Bengio, 2016) addresses that narrower question by asking what a simple readout can recover without changing the encoder. The central question is whether frozen DNA representations carry gene-level functional signal beyond local nucleotide composition, and whether that signal depends on the sequence context supplied to the encoder. A canonical coding sequence contains protein-domain and family information, whereas a long window around a transcription start site samples mostly noncoding sequence around the gene’s promoter. A useful representation for one substrate need not expose the same signal from the other. This framing is distinct from recent DNA-language modelling work. Omni-DNA uses autoregressive genomic pretraining followed by multi-task fine-tuning for tasks including DNA-to-function text generation, while shared-tokenizer and other DNA-language fusion methods train alignment or integration modules over DNA-text pairs (Li et al. , 2025, 2026). These systems show that sequence-text modelling is feasible, but they add substantial multimodal training. GenePT instead represents each gene by embedding a natural-language summary, providing a text-derived gene vector that can be used as a cross-modal target (Chen and Zou, 2023). We use that target to test lightweight linear recoverability from frozen DNA embeddings rather than to train a new DNA-text alignment model. Here, we introduce MINA (Model Interrogation of Nucleotide Architectures), a lightweight probing benchmark for frozen nucleotide encoders, with composition, pooling, and sequence-context controls. MINA tests four frozen self-supervised DNA encoders on two readouts: 5-way protein-family classification, and Ridge regression into the 1,536-d GenePT natural-language target. The corpus contains 3,244 human protein-coding genes from transcription-factor, GPCR, kinase, ion-channel, and immune-receptor families. CDS experiments include a 4-mer compositional baseline, shuffled-label anti-baselines, and a pooling sweep across encoder summaries. We then compare coding sequence with 196,608 bp windows centred on transcription start sites (TSS), using matched TSS 4-mers, all four encoders evaluated on the same TSS windows, and Enformer (Avsec et al. , 2021) as a supervised sequence-to-function comparator. The evidence we report is therefore for two specific things: the protein-family annotation recovered from CDS, and the GenePT natural-language target partially recovered from CDS; we do not claim recovery of arbitrary gene function from noncoding regulatory context.

## 2 Methods 

Dataset. We curated 3,244 human protein-coding genes from five functional families: transcription factors (1,743), G-protein-coupled receptors (591), protein kinases (558), ion channels (198), and immune receptors (154). Genes were selected by regex match against the HGNC 

gene_group field (Seal et al. , 2023), intersected with the GenePT v2 gene-symbol set (Chen and Zou, 2023), and deduped first-family-wins in the assignment order kinase → transcription factor → ion channel →

GPCR → immune receptor (so a gene matching multiple HGNC groups is counted under the earliest-priority family; e.g., a HIPK kinase that also has a TF role is counted as a kinase). The Family column in Table 1 is therefore size-ranked but not assignment-ranked. Canonical coding sequences (CDS) were retrieved from Ensembl (Harrison et al. , 2024). The corpus was split 70/15/15 stratified by family with seed 42 and reused for all downstream runs (Table 1). Of the 18,836 HGNC protein-coding genes covered by GenePT v2, 3,247 match one of the five family regexes and 3,244 of those have a retrievable Ensembl CDS, so the corpus sits at 99.9% of the within-five-families ceiling. We do not extend to the remaining ∼15 ,600 GenePT-covered protein-coding genes because weaker-domain families would likely shrink the encoder-versus-k-mer gap (§5.2). That scope is a separate study.

Encoders. We compared four frozen self-supervised DNA encoders on canonical CDS. DNABERT-2 (Zhou et al. , 2024) is a 117 M-parameter BERT-style model ( zhihan1996/DNABERT-2-117M ) with byte-pair encoding, 768-d hidden states, a 512-token context window, and [CLS] / [SEP] boundary tokens. Nucleotide Transformer v2 multi-species 100M (Dalla-Torre et al. , 2025) ( InstaDeepAI/ nucleotide-transformer-v2-100m-multi-species , hereafter NT-v2) is an ESM-style masked-language model with fixed non-overlapping 6-mer tokens, 512-d hidden states, a 2,048-token context window, and a <cls> marker. GENA-LM base (Fishman et al. ,2025) ( AIRI-Institute/gena-lm-bert-base-t2t ) is a 110 M-parameter BERT-12L masked-language model with 768-d hidden states, 12 attention heads, a 32,000-token BPE vocabulary, and a 512-token context window corresponding to roughly 4.5 kb after BPE compression; the t2t checkpoint was trained on the human T2T assembly with 1000 Genomes SNP augmentation. HyenaDNA large (Nguyen et al. ,2023) (LongSafari/hyenadna-large-1m-seqlen-hf ) is an attention-free causal next-nucleotide model based on Hyena sequence operators, with single-nucleotide tokens, 8 layers, 256-d hidden states, approximately 6.6 M trainable parameters, and a context window up to 1M nucleotides; HyenaDNA was pretrained on the human reference genome. All encoder weights were frozen. 

Encoding and pooling. Canonical CDS were chunked to fit each encoder’s context window, wrapped with model-standard boundary tokens where applicable ( [CLS] /[SEP] for DNABERT-2 and GENA-LM, 

<cls> for NT-v2; HyenaDNA has no learned summary token), and encoded. Because each gene must then be represented by a fixed-size vector, we evaluated simple parameter-free pooling rules rather than training an additional sequence aggregator. Mean, max, and CLS pooling are standard reductions for contextual encoder embeddings (Devlin et al. ,2019; Reimers and Gurevych, 2019); specialmean retains model boundary/special-token positions; and meanD and meanG add coarse first/last/global summaries so long genes are not represented only by an unordered average.

TSS context and Enformer comparator. Non-coding regulatory sequence can carry gene-function signal that protein-domain motifs do not: genes in the same co-regulated module share promoter and enhancer motifs (e.g., NF-κB binding sites near immune-response genes, ISRE elements near interferon-stimulated genes), and that shared regulatory structure is plausibly mirrored in natural-language descriptions of pathway co-membership. The TSS arm therefore tests two related but distinct hypotheses: whether regulatory context recovers the same discrete protein-family label as CDS, and whether it recovers the continuous GenePT natural-language target that is more directly aligned with co-regulated-function vocabulary. To test whether the same family signal is recoverable from TSS-window sequence context, we constructed 196,608 bp genomic windows centred on each gene’s strand-specific transcription start site. These windows are much larger and mostly noncoding rather than protein-domain-rich, so we expected weaker family-label recovery than from CDS while gaining a direct CDS-versus-TSS comparison. We ran all four self-supervised DNA encoders (NT-v2, DNABERT-2, GENA-LM, HyenaDNA) over the same 196,608 bp TSS windows with the CDS pooling rules and probe protocol, plus a matched TSS-window 4-mer baseline; for each encoder we report the best-pool result against the TSS 4-mer baseline. We also included Enformer (Avsec et al. , 2021) as a supervised TSS-to-function comparator rather than a CDS encoder. Enformer takes 196,608 bp DNA input and predicts functional genomic tracks over 896 central 128-bp bins, with 5,313 human and 1,643 mouse output tracks, making it a natural baseline for long TSS-window sequence features. From each Enformer run we computed three summaries for the same probe recipe: trunk_global (mean across all 896 bins), 

trunk_center (mean across the central 16 bins around the TSS, 2,048 bp total), and tracks_center (mean predicted track values over the same central bins). 

Probes. For classification we trained multinomial logistic regression. For the 1,536-d GenePT text-embedding target we trained multi-output ridge regression; ridge was chosen over reduced-rank or partial-least-squares variants for its closed-form efficiency on the high-dimensional target and its alignment with the standard linear-probing protocol. Regularisation ( α

for ridge, C for logistic) was swept on the validation split over a decade grid, the probe was refit on the train+val concatenation at the selected hyperparameter, and metrics were evaluated once on the held-out test split. Linear probing follows the protocol of Alain and Bengio (2016). 

Baselines and controls. The k-mer composition baseline projects each CDS, or each TSS window for the context ablation, to a 256-d L1-normalised 4-mer histogram and runs the same probe recipe; it is encoder-independent and serves as the compositional floor. The shuffled-label anti-baseline permutes family labels in train+val only (test labels remain real) and tests pipeline honesty: chance-corrected agreement should collapse to zero. For the regression probes, an analogous shuffled-Y anti-baseline 

permutes the 1,536-d GenePT vectors in train+val only and re-runs the full 

α sweep on the shuffled validation split (rather than reusing the α selected from the real run), so any non-trivial test R2 would imply leakage in either the data split or the hyperparameter selection. 

Evaluation metrics. For 5-way family classification we report macro-F1 and Cohen’s κ (Cohen, 1960) jointly: macro-F1 weights each family equally and is appropriate for the imbalanced family distribution, while 

κ is the chance-corrected agreement metric. The two carry different information: κ rewards majority-class agreement under imbalance, whereas macro-F1 rewards minority-class recovery, so we report both for every classification cell. Accuracy and confusion matrices are reported as supporting diagnostics; classification deltas are reported relative to the CDS or TSS 4-mer baseline as appropriate. For Ridge-to-GenePT regression we report macro-averaged held-out R2 across the 1,536 GenePT dimensions and mean cosine similarity between predicted and target GenePT vectors, with R2 deltas relative to the CDS 4-mer baseline. For headline cells we report 95% confidence intervals from a 1,000-iteration bootstrap of the held-out test split (stratified by family for classification, i.i.d. by gene for regression); these intervals quantify test-composition sampling uncertainty, not hyperparameter or split-seed variability. 

## 3 Results 

3.1 CDS encoders separate gene families 

On canonical CDS, NT-v2 is the strongest encoder for 5-way family classification: meanD pooling reaches macro-F1 0.828 (95% bootstrap CI [0.77, 0.87]) / Cohen’s κ 0.821 ([0.77, 0.86]), a ∆κ of +0.119 over the CDS 4-mer baseline (macro-F1 0.672 [0.60, 0.72] / κ 0.702 [0.64, 0.75]; Table 2). The gain is not just majority-family recovery: per-class F1 improves most for the minority immune and ion-channel classes ( +0.33 and +0.29 over 4-mers). The shuffled-label anti-baseline collapses to macro-F1 0.208 / κ 0.048, so the family signal is not split leakage or probe bookkeeping. HyenaDNA is competitive with DNABERT-2, while GENA-LM base falls below CDS 4-mer composition; thus, “DNA language model” is not a sufficient explanation for success.

3.2 Cross-modal alignment is weaker than family classification 

Linear projection into the 1,536-d GenePT text-embedding space recovers a smaller signal than family classification (Table 3). DNABERT-2 meanG 

is the best Ridge probe ( R2 0.210, 95% bootstrap CI [0.19, 0.23]), only +0.036 above the CDS 4-mer baseline ( R2 0.174 [0.15, 0.19]). NT-v2 reaches R2 0.193, HyenaDNA 0.182, and GENA-LM 0.117, so the strongest CDS family separator is not necessarily the representation most aligned with text-summary geometry. Cosine similarity is much less discriminative: the shuffled-Y anti-baseline still reaches 0.913, and all real feature sets fall in a narrow 0.925–0.934 band. This compression is consistent with anisotropy in the GenePT embedding space, where many predicted vectors share a common direction. Thus, cosine is useful as a sanity check, but R2 carries the meaningful separation among GenePT probes.

The modest R2 values constrain the biological claim: GenePT summaries are heterogeneous and not limited to protein-family membership. They are also broadly rather than narrowly carried: for DNABERT-2 meanG CDS, 1,527 of the 1,536 GenePT dimensions have positive test R2, the median per-dim R2 is 0.190 (within 0.02 of the 0.210 macro mean), and recovering 50% of the summed R2 requires the top 429 dimensions (28% of the embedding space); the other three encoders show the same broad shape one band lower (Supplementary Figure A1). The headline cross-modal signal is therefore distributed across most of the GenePT space rather than concentrated in a handful of well-predicted summary axes. 3.3 Pooling controls representation geometry 

Pooling choice changes apparent representation quality (Figure 4). The heatmap uses macro-F1 because the sweep compares 5-way classification under class imbalance, and macro-F1 weights each family equally. NT-v2 spans macro-F1 0.586 ( maxmean ) to 0.828 ( meanD ); HyenaDNA’s 

clsmean collapses to macro-F1 0.140 / κ 0 because the causal model has no BERT-style trained summary token, while its concat-style 

meanG reaches 0.715. Concat-style summaries ( meanD and meanG )help both NT-v2 and DNABERT-2, whereas specialmean is nearly indistinguishable from meanmean on CDS. The full encoder × pooling matrix is in Supplementary Table A1; Tables 2 and 3 report best-cell-per-encoder selections from this 24-cell sweep. The model-specific pattern is consistent with each encoder’s training interface: masked-language models expose boundary or summary positions, whereas HyenaDNA is causal and has no trained CLS token. Pooling is therefore part of the representation interface, not a neutral implementation detail.

3.4 Genomic context controls recoverable family signal 

Holding the gene set fixed and replacing the CDS substrate with 196,608 bp TSS-centred windows collapses both readouts across every self-supervised encoder we tested (Table 4). Each encoder’s classification κ

drops by roughly half between CDS and TSS, and each encoder’s R2

drops to roughly half its CDS value; the substrate effect is encoder-general rather than an NT-v2 artefact. Every encoder still beats the matched TSS 4-mer baseline on both metrics, so the TSS windows are not uninformative; they expose family-correlated promoter or cis-regulatory motifs rather than independent recovery of protein-family information. Supervised Enformer trunk features improve further on TSS ( κ 0.439; R2 0.142) consistent with its supervised long-window training objective, but remain below the CDS encoders on family labels. The benchmark therefore supports a narrower claim: frozen DNA encoders recover coding-sequence family information, with recoverability controlled by genomic substrate uniformly across architectures.

## 4 Discussion 

Supported claim. MINA shows that frozen DNA encoders can expose coding-sequence family signal to a lightweight linear probe. The result is not a trivial success against chance: the CDS 4-mer baseline is already strong, and the shuffled-label anti-baseline collapses as expected. That strong 4-mer floor is itself biologically meaningful, because coding sequences already carry family information through nucleotide composition, codon and amino-acid biases, and short motif structure. NT-v2 nevertheless improves over CDS 4-mers by ∆κ +0.119 on 5-way family classification, indicating family-discriminative structure beyond unordered short-motif composition. The supported claim is therefore narrow: linearly recoverable family signal in canonical CDS, not general gene-function prediction. 

Genomic context. The CDS-versus-TSS ablation is the main guardrail on interpretation because it holds the gene set fixed while changing the input substrate. Across all four self-supervised encoders run on the same 196,608 bp TSS windows the pattern is consistent: every encoder drops by 0.27–0.38 macro-F1 versus its CDS result, and all four cluster in macro-F1 0.39–0.46 on TSS with mutually-overlapping 95% bootstrap CIs. That collapse is biologically coherent: the labels are mostly protein-family labels, whereas TSS windows contain promoter, untranslated, intronic, and broader genomic sequence around each gene. Every encoder still beats the TSS 4-mer baseline (0.247) with non-overlapping CIs, so these windows are not uninformative; they carry indirect family correlates but do not match CDS encoders on protein-family labels. The substrate, not the encoder, is the dominant variable across all four self-supervised DNA encoders, ruling out an NT-v2-specific artefact. Enformer trunk features remain the strongest TSS comparator at macro-F1 0.545, consistent with its supervised long-window training objective. 

Model and pooling specificity. The encoder comparison argues against treating DNA language models as interchangeable feature extractors. NT-v2 is the clearest classification winner, DNABERT-2 is strongest for Ridge-to-GenePT regression, HyenaDNA is competitive on family classification, and GENA-LM base falls below CDS 4-mer composition on both probes. Thus, pretraining alone does not guarantee a linearly useful gene representation on this benchmark. Pooling is also part of the representation interface: concat-style summaries help NT-v2 and DNABERT-2, CLS-style pooling collapses for HyenaDNA, and 

maxmean hurts every encoder tested. A plausible interpretation is that pooling works best when it matches what training made available: BERT-style models expose boundary or summary positions, concat summaries preserve coarse first/last/global information across long CDS chunks, and HyenaDNA has no trained CLS token. These patterns show why frozen-encoder comparisons need explicit pooling controls rather than a single default summary vector. 

Cross-modal signal. Ridge regression into GenePT space gives acomplementary but weaker view of the same representations. The best cross-modal gain is modest ( ∆R2 about +0.04 over CDS 4-mers), and the model ranking differs from classification: DNABERT-2 aligns best with GenePT summary geometry while NT-v2 best separates families. GenePT vectors are an inherently noisy target for a linear sequence probe because they inherit the geometry of English gene summaries, including shared linguistic style, broad biomedical semantics, and many features not directly determined by coding sequence. That shared structure can make vectors point in similar directions, producing high cosine even for shuffled targets, while the sequence-relevant axes remain harder to recover linearly. The compressed cosine values therefore suggest that GenePT-space alignment should be interpreted through R2 rather than raw directional similarity. They also suggest that stronger DNA-to-text alignment may require subspace alignment or a transformed GenePT target that behaves more like a gene-vector space: preserving function-relevant axes while discarding language-embedding variation that is noisy for sequence probing.

## 5 Conclusion and Study Limitations 

5.1 Conclusion 

Frozen DNA encoders can recover coding-sequence family signal beyond simple composition, but the strength and meaning of that signal depend on genomic context, model recipe, pooling, and the target used to define “function.” MINA therefore supports a precise conclusion: frozen DNA representations can be useful linearly readable CDS feature extractors, but these results should not be read as generic recovery of gene function from arbitrary genomic sequence. 

5.2 Study Limitations and Next Steps 

Several limitations follow directly from the design. The corpus is human-only, spans ∼3,200 genes, and uses five broad protein-family categories with strong domain structure; families with weaker sequence signatures may yield smaller encoder-versus-k-mer gaps. The current 3,244 genes already cover 99.9% of the within-five-families GenePT ∩HGNC ceiling (Methods, §Dataset), so the natural next step would be to extend the corpus to all ∼18,836 GenePT-covered protein-coding genes; we treat that scope as a separate study because it replaces the five-family framing with a new ontology and tests a distinct question. Family membership was deduplicated first-family-wins across overlapping HGNC groups, and the split was random-stratified at the gene level rather than clustered by sequence similarity, so paralogous genes can appear on both sides of the split. The TSS context arm covers all four self-supervised encoders plus the supervised Enformer comparator, but is restricted to a single 196,608 bp window centred on the strand-specific TSS, and Enformer’s pretraining tracks include many of the gene loci it is evaluated on, so the substrate-dominance claim is constrained to this window choice and to Enformer-overlapping loci. HyenaDNA is also substantially smaller than the BERT-style encoders (256-d hidden, ∼6.6 M parameters), so its competitive but lower headline result mixes architecture, capacity, and pretraining objective. On the analysis side, the headline 1,000-iteration bootstrap confidence intervals quantify held-out test-composition sampling only; they do not cover split-seed variance, hyperparameter uncertainty, or multiplicity from best-cell-per-encoder reporting across the 24-cell pooling grid. Minority-class test sets are also small (immune n=23 , ion n=30 ), making per-class components noisy. Stronger follow-up studies would use paralog-aware splits (e.g., MMseqs2 or CD-HIT clustering at a stated identity threshold), additional organisms and label sets, broader noncoding targets, per-layer probes, paired permutation tests, and matched context sweeps across architectures and TSS window sizes.

## B Code Reproducibility 

The project repository is available at github.com/Austin-Senna/ dna_to_text . The repository README.md is the canonical entry point for reproducing the analysis. It separates the workflow into six stages: (1) gene curation and canonical CDS retrieval; (2) frozen CDS encoder extraction and pooling-dataset construction; (3) frozen split creation plus family-classification and Ridge-to-GenePT probes; (4) TSS-window construction, NT-v2 TSS extraction, and Enformer comparator features; (5) bootstrap confidence intervals; and (6) report table and figure generation. Large intermediate caches, including raw sequence caches, per-gene encoder reductions, Enformer windows, and source GenePT artifacts, are generated locally and ignored by git; report-facing metrics, tables, figures, split metadata, and selected feature/probe caches are tracked. 

B.1 Reviewer-facing reproduction path 

The fastest report-supporting reproduction path starts from the tracked caches and does not rerun the expensive encoder extraction. From the repository root, the README gives:  

> •

Stage 5: run scripts/bootstrap_test_uncertainty.py 

to regenerate the 1,000-iteration bootstrap confidence intervals in 

data/bootstrap_metrics.json ; 

> •

Stage 6: run scripts/build_analysis_artifacts.py 

with --overwrite to regenerate report-facing tables, figures, and the artifact manifest from tracked metrics and cached outputs;  

> •

Smoke check: run the same artifact builder with --skip-umap ,

--overwrite , and an output directory such as /tmp/dna_ analysis_smoke .The full public-source rebuild uses the same README stage order: Stage 1 builds the curated gene table and fetches CDS; Stage 2 runs the four frozen DNA encoders and materializes pooling datasets; Stage 3 trains probes and baselines; Stage 4 derives TSS-centred windows and runs NT-v2/Enformer comparisons; Stages 5–6 regenerate uncertainty estimates and report artifacts. The optional Enformer branch requires installing the repository’s .[enformer] extra. 

B.2 Deterministic settings and hyperparameters 

The split and probe settings are fixed in code rather than chosen ad hoc during report writing. scripts/make_splits.py calls 

src/splits/make_splits.py with seed 42, stratifying on the 

family column. The resulting data/splits.json contains 2,270 train genes, 487 validation genes, and 487 held-out test genes from 

data/dataset.parquet . All downstream probe scripts read this same split file. Classification probes use multinomial logistic regression with the scikit-learn lbfgs solver, max_iter=2000 , and an L2 regularisation sweep over {10 −2, 10 −1, 1, 10 , 100 , 1000 } for C. The selected C

maximises validation macro-F1. Ridge-to-GenePT probes sweep α ∈{10 −2, 10 −1, 1, 10 , 100 , 1000 }, selecting by validation mean cosine. After selection, both probe families are refit on train+validation and evaluated once on the held-out test split. Shuffled-label and shuffled-GenePT anti-baselines permute train+validation targets only, using seed 42, while test labels or targets remain real. Bootstrap confidence intervals are regenerated by scripts/ bootstrap_test_uncertainty.py with default --n-iters 

1000 and --seed 42. Classification resampling is stratified by family; regression resampling is i.i.d. by gene. These intervals therefore quantify held-out test-composition sampling uncertainty only, not split-seed, hyperparameter, or encoder-extraction variability. Encoder extraction is deterministic up to backend numerical differences and is run with frozen model weights in evaluation/inference mode. The default --device auto selects CUDA, then Apple Silicon MPS, then CPU. The registered CDS chunk settings are: DNABERT-2, 510 content tokens with stride 64; NT-v2, 998 content tokens with stride 64; GENA-LM, 510 content tokens with stride 64; and HyenaDNA, 8,192 content tokens with stride 512. Pooling datasets are generated from cached per-chunk reductions for meanmean , specialmean ,

maxmean , clsmean , meanD , and meanG where supported by the encoder. TSS windows are 196,608 bp, centred on the strand-aware Ensembl transcription start site. For Enformer summaries, the default central-window setting averages the central 16 128-bp bins, i.e. 2,048 bp around the TSS. UMAP diagnostics use random_state=42 , n_ neighbors=15 , and min_dist=0.1 .
