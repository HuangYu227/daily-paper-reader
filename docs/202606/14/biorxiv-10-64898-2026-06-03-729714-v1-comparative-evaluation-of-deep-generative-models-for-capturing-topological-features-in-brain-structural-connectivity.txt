Abstract 39 

Structural connectivity (SC) data are crucial for brain network analysis, but SC -based machine learning 40 

often su=ers from limited data availability, hindering model generalization and robustness. Although 41 

data augmentation using deep generative models has attracted increasing attention, it remains unclear 42 

how di=erent models capture the complex topological features of SC data. To clarify the learning 43 

characteristics of deep generative models for SC generation, this study compares three representative 44 

models: variational autoencoder (VAE), Wasserstein GAN with gradient penalty (WGAN -GP), and 45 

denoising di=usion probabilistic models (DDPM). We systematically evaluated these models using both 46 

synthetic datasets with known characteristics and real -world SC data. Generation quality was assessed 47 

using graph -theoretic metric comparisons and visual inspection of the generated adjacency matrices. 48 

WGAN -GP showed relatively stable performance across datasets and metrics, without severe 49 

performance degradation across evaluation settings. In contrast, VAE and DDPM performed well in 50 

specific aspects but were more sensitive to data characteristics. These findings suggest that WGAN -GP 51 

may serve as the most balanced baseline for future SC data augmentation studies, whereas VAE and 52 

DDPM may be useful depending on the target application and structural properties of interest. 53 

Furthermore, because all models struggled to fully reproduce strict global constraints such as planarity, 54 

our results suggest that standard generative models may be insu=icient to capture the complex 55 

topological features of SC data . This highlights the importance of incorporating the desired structural 56 

properties into the training or generation process. 57

# 1. Introduction 61 

Recently, brain structural connectivity (SC) has gained significant attention in neuroscience 62 research due to its potential to reveal insights into brain function and organization. 3,4 There has been 63 a growing interest in applying SC to various applications, including investigations into brain 64 development and aging, and analysis of neurological and brain disorders. 5 Specifically, machine 65 learning approaches based on SC have been utilized to diagnose and understand disorders such as 66 Alzheimer’s disease 6 and multiple sclerosis. 7 However, the practical application of SC-based 67 models often sugers from limited data availability, which agects the generalization and robustness 68 of machine learning approaches. Acquiring large-scale dMRI datasets is often challenging due to 69 high costs and time-consuming data collection processes. 8 Neuroimaging studies frequently suger 70 from limited sample sizes, which introduces bias in training data and limits the generalizability of 71 machine learning models. 9 Such data scarcity often leads to overfitting and unreliable model 72 performance in the downstream machine learning analyses, such as prediction or diagnosis. 73 Although data augmentation is an egective strategy for addressing data scarcity, traditional 74 augmentation methods such as rotation or flipping are not applicable to SC data. Because SC data 75 is represented as an adjacency matrix where each row and column corresponds to a specific brain 76 region, such geometric transformations would destroy this correspondence. Therefore, more 77 sophisticated approaches that preserve the topological structure of SC data are required for 78 egective data augmentation. Brain structural networks are characterized by complex topological 79 features, such as short path length, high clustering coegicient, hub presence, and modularity, 80 alongside functionally critical, strong edge weights. 3,10 These features fundamentally facilitate the 81 egicient segregation and integration of information. Thus, generating biologically plausible SC data 82 requires capturing the high-dimensional topological dependencies among multiple regions, a task 83 that is inherently challenging for simple approaches. 84 To overcome this limitation, deep generative models have been explored for SC data augmentation, 85 though a comprehensive understanding of their comparative capabilities remains lacking. By 86 learning the underlying distribution of the training data, these models generate new samples that 87 resemble the original data. Representative models such as variational autoencoders (VAE) 11 and 88 generative adversarial networks (GANs) 12 have been successfully applied to generate synthetic SC 89 data, demonstrating clear improvements in downstream tasks. 13 –15 Additionally, digusion models 16 

90 have emerged as a promising approach for generating high-quality synthetic data across various 91 domains. While their direct application to SC data remains underexplored, researchers have begun 92 exploring digusion models for brain connectivity, such as functional connectivity (FC) generation 93 and bidirectional SC-FC mapping, 17,18 making digusion models an important emerging candidate 94 for SC generation as well. However, previous studies have primarily focused on individual generative 95 models for SC data augmentation, using digerent datasets and evaluation metrics. This makes it 96 challenging to directly compare the ability of digerent generative models to capture the graph 97 topology in this context. Given that VAEs, GANs, and digusion models rely on fundamentally 98 digerent generative paradigms, each model is expected to exhibit distinct behaviors when 99 reconstructing SC structures. Addressing this gap and revealing how each paradigm learns brain 100 network characteristics is a crucial step toward understanding the fundamental capabilities and 101 limitations of deep generative models in this domain. 102 To elucidate how deep generative models learn and reproduce the topological features of SC data, 103 this study evaluates and compares three representative generative models: VAE, Wasserstein GAN 104

# 2. Methods 114 

In this study, we investigated the performance of three generative models for augmenting brain SC 115 data. To comprehensively evaluate performance against SC data, this study employed both 116 synthetic and real-world SC datasets. Since real-world SC data is complex and has an ambiguous 117 ground truth, evaluating solely on real SC data makes it digicult to identify which structural features 118 the model struggles to reproduce. To address this, our evaluation framework first isolates and 119 assesses the ability to reproduce individual features using single, specific network characteristics. 120 Subsequently, we evaluate the models on real-world SC data, where these features are combined. 121 An overview of the experimental framework used in this study is illustrated in Figure 1, which 122 includes the datasets, the compared models, and the evaluation protocols. 123 124 Figure 1 Overview of the entire methods used in this study. 125

2.1.1. Variational Autoencoder 131 

VAE, proposed by Kingma and Welling, 11 is a generative model that learns to encode input data into 132 a latent space and decode samples from this space back to the data space. The model consists of 133 two neural networks: an encoder and a decoder. The encoder maps the input data 𝑥 into a latent 134 representation 𝑧 , while the decoder reconstructs the original data from the latent representation. 135 The model is trained to minimize the sum of a reconstruction loss and a Kullback-Leibler (KL) 136 divergence loss. 137 

In this study, both the encoder and the decoder were fully connected neural networks that took the 138 flattened lower-triangular part of the adjacency matrix as input and produced the output. 139 

2.1.2. Wasserstein GAN with Gradient Penalty 140 

WGAN-GP is a variant of GANs that improves training stability by using the Wasserstein distance as 141 a loss function and adding a gradient penalty term, as proposed by Arjovsky et al. 20 and further 142 refined by Gulrajani et al. 19 The model consists of two neural networks: a generator and a critic. The 143 generator creates synthetic data from random noise, while the critic evaluates the authenticity of 144 the given data, which can be either real or generated. 145 In this study, both the generator and the critic were fully connected neural networks that processed 146 the flattened lower-triangular part of the adjacency matrix. 147 

2.1.3. Denoising DiIusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) 148 

DDPM, proposed by Ho et al. 16 is a generative model that learns to convert random noise into data 149 through a series of denoising steps. The model has two processes: a forward digusion process and 150 a reverse denoising process. In the forward process, Gaussian noise is added to the data over a 151 series of time steps. A neural network is trained to predict the noise added to the original data, given 152 the noisy data and the time step. In the reverse process, the model generates data from random 153 noise by iteratively removing the predicted noise. 154 For the implementation of the DDPM, we adopted the standard U-Net 21 architecture. Although the 155 other evaluated models employ fully connected networks, the U-Net is the established de facto 156 standard for digusion models. To conduct a fair and realistic evaluation of DDPM’s foundational 157 capabilities without bottlenecking its performance through an MLP architecture, we followed this 158 standard practice. The number of the noising and denoising steps was set to 𝑇 = 1000 as in the 159 original paper. 160 

# 2.2. Datasets 161 

Generating realistic SC data requires reproducing topological features inherent in brain networks, 162 such as short path lengths, high clustering coegicients, hub presence, and modularity. 3 These 163 features are crucial for brain function, since they fundamentally facilitate the egicient segregation 164 and integration of information. Specifically, small-worldness, which refers to the coexistence of 165 short path lengths and high clustering coegicients, balances specialized local processing with 166 egective global communication at a lower cost. 22 Modularity, which refers to the organization of the 167 network with densely connected clusters internally and sparsely connected across clusters, 168 supports specialized regional processing. Hubs act as central nodes for information exchange, 169

To 173 evaluate whether the generative models can capture these foundational elements, we selected five 174 distinct synthetic datasets. 2.2.1. Synthetic Datasets 177 

We selected five types of synthetic datasets to reproduce a range of graph structures and properties. 178 The size of the synthetic datasets was fixed at 64 nodes. Detailed generation procedures and 179 parameter settings for each synthetic dataset are provided in the Supplementary Information. 180 

Barabási -Albert ( 𝑁 = 500 )181 

We utilized the BA dataset to investigate the reproduction of specific degree distributions and hub 182 nodes. BA models generate scale-free networks featuring few high-degree hubs and many low-183 degree nodes. Although brain networks exhibit an exponential degree distribution rather than strictly 184 scale-free, 23,24 we selected the BA dataset because its ability to reproduce hub structures and 185 degree distributions is highly relevant to capturing the fundamental properties of SC. 186 

Planar ( 𝑁 = 200 )187 

The planar dataset benchmarks for evaluating the ability to learn global topological constraints. 188 Planar graphs have a strict global rule that edges must not cross in 2-dimensional space; therefore, 189 investigating whether the generated graphs meet planarity constraints gives insights into a model’s 190 capacity to learn implicit global rules. We used a planar dataset from Martinkus et al. 1

191 

Stochastic Block Model ( 𝑁 = 500 )192 

The SBM dataset was selected to assess the model’s ability to reproduce community structure. SBM 193 graphs consist of distinct communities with random intra- and inter-connections, making them 194 suitable for evaluating community reproduction. Since the brain has an explicit modularity, 195 evaluating the reproduction of these structures is critical for SC generation. 196 

Watts -Strogatz ( 𝑁 = 500 )197 

The WS dataset was chosen to evaluate the models’ capability to capture small-worldness. 198 Because small-worldness is a fundamental property of brain networks that enables egicient 199 information transfer, assessing its reproducibility is essential for validating generative models. 200 

Weighted Scale -Free ( 𝑁 = 500 )201 

We selected a weighted scale-free network to assess the models’ capability to generate weighted 202 graphs. Since SC data forms a weighted network, generating not only the correct binary structure 203 but also precise edge weights is important for reflecting the foundation backbone. We generated 204 500 weighted scale-free graphs using the method proposed by Yook et al. 25

2.2.2. Structural Connectivity Dataset 206 

To evaluate the generative models on complex real-world brain connectivity data, we utilized an 207 empirical SC dataset. The dataset was derived from digusion MRI and T1-weighted MRI data of 208 healthy adult participants, originally obtained from the NIMH Healthy Volunteer Dataset 209 (https://doi.org/10.18112/openneuro.ds004215.v1.0.0). 2

210 The MRI data preprocessing and structural connectivity mapping were performed as previously 211 described. 26 Briefly, digusion and T1-weighted images were preprocessed using QSIprep (0.15.4) 27 

212 pipeline. Probabilistic tractography (iFOD2) combined with SIFT2 was applied, and the whole brain 213 was parcellated into 116 regions based on the automated anatomical labeling (AAL) atlas to 214 construct the structural connectivity matrices. For detailed procedures for DWI denoising, 215 distortion correction, and fiber orientation estimation, please refer to the previous study. 26 In total, 216 we used data from 107 participants for our experiments. 217 

# 2.3. Training Setup 218 

We evaluated the performance of the three generative models on the six datasets described above. 219 Each model was trained separately on each of the five synthetic datasets and on one real brain 220 structural connectivity (SC) dataset. 221 

2.3.1. Data Preprocessing 222 

For the synthetic datasets, node ordering was fixed based on dataset-specific criteria, motivated by 223 the fact that SC data have a consistent node ordering across samples, meaning that they are not 224 permutation-invariant. To ensure consistent node ordering in the synthetic datasets, we applied the 225 following methods: Depth-First Search (DFS) for BA, Planar, and WSF datasets; k-core ordering for 226 WS dataset; and community-based k-core ordering for SBM dataset. 227 Regarding edge features, SC edge weights represent streamline counts, and their maximum values 228 vary significantly across individuals and acquisition environments, making standard uniform 229 scaling problematic. Consequently, we refrained from applying any scaling procedures to the SC 230 data. To maintain a consistent evaluation framework, we also omitted scaling for all synthetic 231 datasets. While we acknowledge that generative models typically benefit from input data being 232 scaled to a specific optimal range for stable training, we prioritized preserving the inherent 233 distribution and variance of the unscaled connectivity data. 234

Synthetic datasets 236 

For each dataset, we randomly split the data into training, validation, and test sets in a 0.6:0.2:0.2 237 ratio. 238 

Structural Connectivity dataset 239 

The SC dataset was split into discovery and test sets at a 3:1 ratio. The discovery dataset was further 240 split into training and validation sets at a 3:1 ratio. 241 The dataset was split so that fluid intelligence scores were evenly distributed across the sets. These 242

# 2.4. Evaluation Metrics 252 

To evaluate how generative models learn the aforementioned topological features essential for 253 brain networks, we selected the following evaluation metrics. The evaluation included both metrics 254 common to all datasets and metrics defined specifically for individual datasets. 255 

2.4.1. Edge Thresholding 256 

For the synthetic datasets, all graphs were thresholded to achieve an edge density similar to that of 257 the training dataset before evaluation, since some models tended to generate subtle edges rather 258 than no edges. The thresholded value was searched in increments of 0.01 to minimize the digerence 259 in edge density between the generated graphs and the whole original dataset. Non-weighted graphs 260 were binarized after thresholding. 261 For the SC dataset, we applied percentile-based thresholding to the edge weights of the generated 262 graphs. This approach was employed to focus on the important connections in the brain network. 263 We retained the top 5%, 10%, 20%, and 30% of the strongest edges in each generated graph for most 264 evaluation metrics. However, for the strong edge preservation metric, we retained the top 1%, 5%, 265 and 10% of the strongest edges to assess the models’ ability to reconstruct the most significant 266 connections. For the hub preservation metric, no thresholding was applied, as node strength can 267 be calculated without thresholding. 268 

2.4.2. Common Metrics 269 

MMD -based Evaluation 270 

Following previous studies, 1,32 we used MMD to quantitatively assess the similarity between the test 271 and generated datasets. Because MMD measures the distance between two underlying probability 272 distributions based on finite samples, it allows us to evaluate the overall characteristics of 273 statistical graph properties rather than relying on a single representative value. Specifically, we 274 computed the MMD for the following graph-theoretical features: node degree, clustering coegicient, 275 orbit counts, the spectrum of the graph Laplacian matrix, and graph embeddings. 276 Since MMD values are relative and depend on the specific datasets and features being compared, 277 we calculated reference MMD values to provide context for interpreting the results. For the BA, SBM, 278 WS, and WSF datasets, we generated new graphs using the same generation process as the original 279 dataset to compute reference MMD values. For the planar and SC datasets, the reference values 280 were calculated using the training and validation sets, since their generation processes were not 281

2.4.3. Dataset -specific Metrics 289 

To evaluate whether the generative models successfully captured the intrinsic topological 290 properties unique to each dataset, we employed specific metrics that quantify their essential 291 structural characteristics. 292 

Validity 293 

For the Planar dataset, the validity of the generated graphs was evaluated by whether they had a 294 planar structure, that is, the absence of edge crossings in 2-dimensional space. It was calculated 295 to determine whether the generative models could implicitly learn the dataset's global structural 296 constraints. 297 

Modularity 298 

Modularity was calculated to assess community structure, a feature of both the SBM and SC 299 datasets. Modularity quantifies the degree to which a network is partitioned into distinct clusters, 300 with internal edge density higher than external (inter-community) connections. 301 We detected communities using Clauset-Newman-Moore greedy modularity maximization, 302 calculated the modularity of the detected communities, and compared the modularity distributions 303 between the generated and test datasets by computing the Wasserstein distance between them. 304 

Small -worldness 305 

Since the WS and SC datasets are characterized by small-world properties, the small-worldness 306 coegicient 𝜎 of each graph was calculated, following the definition proposed by Humphries and 307 Gurney. 33 This metric was chosen to determine if the generative models can reproduce the balance 308 between local dense connections while maintaining global egicient paths, which are 309 characteristics of brain networks. We compared the distributions of 𝜎 values between the 310 generated and test datasets and calculated the Wasserstein distance between them. 311 

Node Strength MMD 312 

For the WSF and SC datasets, MMD of node strength was calculated to evaluate the models’ ability 313 to reproduce the weighted graph. Node strength, defined as the sum of the weights of all edges 314 connected to a node, is a pivotal metric that integrates topological connectivity with weight intensity. 315 Evaluating node strength reveals the structural rules governing how weights are distributed across 316 the network, such as the concentration of high-intensity connections on specific functional hubs. 317 

Strong Edge/Hub Preservation 318 

To further evaluate the biological plausibility of the generated brain networks, we focused on the 319 preservation of strong edges and hubs consistently observed across individuals. By focusing on the 320 mean adjacency matrix, we specifically assess whether the models can reconstruct the stable, 321 fundamental backbone of the brain network that persists despite individual variations. 322 For strong edge reconstruction, we identified the top 𝑛 % (𝑛 = 1, 5, 10) strongest edges in the mean 323 

adjacency matrix of the test dataset and compared them with those of the generated graphs using 324

# 3. Results 329 

# 3.1. Synthetic Datasets 330 

We qualitatively evaluated the generated results for synthetic datasets using mean adjacency 331 matrices (Figure 2) and individual matrices (Figure 3). All models exhibited limitations in reproducing 332 long-range connections (e.g., og-diagonal edges in BA, Planar, WS) and weighted edges (WSF). The 333 residual plot for WSF showed large positive values, showing the underestimation of edge weights 334 across all architectures. 335 VAE replicated high-probability patterns (e.g., edges near the diagonal lines in BA, Planar) but 336 exhibits a bias towards over-representing common patterns, including hub-related edges in BA and 337 intra-community edges in SBM. Consequently, generated graphs lacked the sparsity and long-range 338 connections observed in test data. 339 DDPM, in contrast, excels at reproducing random distributions (e.g., SBM blocks), which was 340 evident in the BA, Planar and WSF datasets. However, it lacked clear diagonal structures 341 characteristic of the Planar and WSF datasets and occasionally failed to generate distinct patterns 342 entirely. 343 WGAN-GP showed balanced ability to reproduce common patterns and generate random edges, 344 capturing the clear patterns without over-emphasizing on common edges. While WGAN-GP 345 generated the more visually plausible compared to the other models, continuous structures in the 346 Planar and WS datasets were fragmented into scattered edges. 347

# 4. Discussion 433 

# 4.1. Synthetic Graphs Reveal Distinct Generative Biases Across Models 434 

The synthetic datasets were used not to directly determine the best model for SC data, but to 435 decompose graph generation into controlled structural properties and to identify model-specific 436 generative biases. Here, we discuss the three major tendencies found: common limitations of 437 standard generative models in reproducing strict graph constraints and weighted edges; 438 architecture-dependent digerences in preserving coordinate-specific adjacency patterns; and 439 paradigm-dependent biases in how each model balances common structures, stochastic 440 variability, and global distributional matching. 441 

4.1.1. Shared Limitations in Reproducing Global Constraints and Weighted Edges 442 

The digiculty of reproducing strict global constraints, which require macroscopic consistency 443 regarding the presence of edges across the entire network, suggests that simple generative models 444 rely on optimizing statistical resemblance and may struggle to implicitly learn the complex 445 topological rules that govern these properties. All models struggled to capture the degree 446 distribution of BA (Figure 4A) and the validity of Planar (VAE: 0.11, WGAN-GP: 0.0030, DDPM: 447 0.0010; Figure 4A). Similarly, capturing the small-world property of WS posed a universal challenge 448 for all models, primarily due to the digiculty of generating sparse long-range connections and the 449 topological constraints. The fact that all models showed smaller 𝜎 values (Figure 4C) and visually 450 plausible local connections indicates a bias that prioritizes local connectivity over the generation of 451

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 8, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.03.729714 doi: bioRxiv preprint

17 rare, long-range shortcuts. While WGAN-GP and DDPM generated og-diagonal edges visually 452 (Figure 3D), the 𝜎 discrepancy indicates that these did not serve as structurally precise shortcuts to 453 reduce global path length. Ultimately, relying solely on optimizing statistical resemblance without 454 explicit rules impedes learning higher-order edge dependencies required for global structural 455 consistency. 456 The transition to weighted graphs significantly revealed a critical, shared limitation across all 457 models. Performance deteriorated significantly in WSF compared to structurally similar unweighted 458 BA graphs (Figure 4A), with all architectures underestimating edge weights (Figure 2E). We attribute 459 this performance drop primarily to the ambiguity in continuous matrices. In binary graphs, small-460 weight edges can be treated as non-edges after thresholding. However, in weighted graphs, a low 461 value should represent valid “weak connections”, introducing ambiguity between meaningful 462 connections and background noise. Accurately reproducing continuous weights alongside 463 topological sparsity, therefore, remains a shared, unresolved challenge for these generative 464 baselines. These shared limitations highlight the need to carefully select a baseline based on its 465 specific generative biases rather than expecting perfect reproduction. 466 

4.1.2. Architecture -Dependent Preservation of Coordinate -Specific Patterns 467 

The inherent inductive biases of the network architectures, i.e., MLP versus CNN. Fundamentally 468 dictate the models’ capabilities in reproducing rigid, coordinate-specific graph patterns. As 469 observed in the Planar and WSF datasets, MLP-based models (VAE and WGAN-GP) successfully 470 maintained high-probability, coordinate-specific patterns (Figure 3B, E). Conversely, DDPM 471 struggled with the rigid coordinate-specific patterns, resulting in the disappearance of the required 472 fixed edges near the diagonal. This discrepancy can be attributed to the ways these architectures 473 process data. MLPs process the entire input simultaneously through fully connected layers, which 474 allows them to learn global relationships between all pairs of nodes and generate pixel-wise similar 475 outputs in common patterns. This aligns with findings in Tabular data generation tasks, where MLP-476 based models are reported to outperform CNN-based models, 39 suggesting that MLPs are better 477 suited for generating data where capturing global relationships is more crucial than local features. 478 In contrast, CNNs rely on translation invariance through shared convolutional filters. While this 479 inductive bias is highly advantageous for natural images, it becomes fundamentally detrimental for 480 node-aligned graph data. In adjacency matrices, each cell represents an edge between specific 481 nodes; thus, a slight spatial shift that would be trivial in an image creates a structurally invalid 482 connection between entirely digerent nodes. Because preserving exact spatial coordinates is 483 required for SC adjacency matrices, these results suggest that MLP-based architectures are better 484 suited to this task than CNN-based models. 485 

4.1.3. Paradigm -Dependent Generative Biases 486 

The VAE consistently exhibited a strong bias towards generating common edges, fundamentally 487 struggling to capture rare structural connections and global constraints. This intrinsic bias was 488 clearly shown in the results. Although VAE achieved the highest scores on 4 of 6 metrics for the 489 Planar dataset (Figure 4A), visual inspection shows an exaggerated concentration of edges around 490 the diagonal and visually implausible graphs (Figure 2B). This same overemphasis led to an inflated 491 modularity of nearly 0.5 in the SBM dataset, due to excessive intra-community connections and 492 fewer inter-community connections, resulting in a loss of crucial long-range shortcuts in WS. For 493 WSF, the VAE generated mean-like outputs that lacked sparse edge patterns, resulting in poor 494

This 496 phenomenon is a well-documented characteristic of VAEs; optimizing the element-wise loss forces 497 the model to predict the average of multiple data points mapped to the same latent representation, 498 leading to over-smoothed outputs in which rare or sharp features are lost. 40,41 

499 DDPM exhibits a distinct advantage in capturing stochastic, texture-like connection patterns, but 500 its fundamental generative mechanisms pose significant challenges when applied to sparse graph 501 matrices. This superiority was evident in the capture of the modularity of SBM. The blurriness 502 observed in the mean adjacency matrix (Figure 2C) signifies DDPM’s capacity to capture the 503 underlying connection probabilities rather than overfitting to rigid edges. Since SBM connections 504 are stochastic, the resulting adjacency matrices resemble structured noise-like textures, aligning 505 with recent research showing digusion models’ egicacy in synthesizing textures with rich spatial 506 dynamics. 34 However, compounding its architectural struggle with rigid constraints, the generative 507 mechanics of DDPMs inherently challenge sparse matrix generation. During the forward digusion 508 process, uniform Gaussian noise rapidly obliterates isolated edges in inherently sparse graph 509 matrices, unlike in natural images, where macroscopic, low-frequency structures typically persist. 510 Consequently, the coarse-to-refine denoising process in DDPMs encounters intrinsic digiculties: 511 lacking continuous local context, the model cannot reliably reproduce the existence of edges, 512 potentially contributing to the loss of structural sharpness observed in individual generated 513 samples. 514 Overall, WGAN-GP demonstrated highly balanced generative performance across the synthetic 515 dataset, which may be attributed to its adversarial training framework. Specifically, the model 516 consistently ranked 1st or 2nd across the evaluated metrics (Figure 4A), avoiding the over-517 smoothing or fixed-coordinate collapse observed in VAE and DDPM. However, it is not without 518 limitations: in addition to the universal challenges, visual inspection reveals that continuous 519 structures were fragmented in the Planar and WS datasets (Figure 3B, D), and the model did not 520 achieve quantitative results comparable to the test dataset. This indicates that while adversarial 521 training, rather than element-wise losses, is robust to global distributional matching, this paradigm 522 remains insugicient to perfectly replicate strict local topological rules. Nevertheless, WGAN-GP 523 remains the most balanced baseline among the evaluated models. 524 

# 4.2. Model -Specific Biases in Empirical SC Generation 525 

The SC results were interpreted in light of the model-specific biases identified in the synthetic 526 datasets. This section discusses how the ability to preserve coordinate-specific patterns, common 527 structural backbones, stochastic variability, and weighted edges translated into empirical SC 528 generation. 529 

4.2.1. Preservation of Coordinate -Specific SC Backbones 530 

As discussed in Section 4.1.2, the MLP architecture’s capacity to preserve fixed-coordinate 531 structural properties directly translates to the successful reconstruction of the stable brain network. 532 The strong pattern retention is visually plausible (Figure 5) and quantitatively supported by high 533 preservation scores for both strong edges and hubs. VAE and WGAN-GP achieved Jaccard indices 534 ranging from 0.88 to 0.91 across all thresholds (Figure 6D) and a near-perfect Spearman correlation 535 coegicient of 0.99 in node strength preservation (Table 2). In the human brain, these densely 536

When evaluated on the top 5% of edges, WGAN-GP 544 exhibited notably low MMD scores across all metrics, comparable to the reference baseline (Figure 545 6A), followed by VAE. This supports that, at the backbone level, these models could reproduce not 546 only the average tendencies but also the precise high-dimensional distribution of each graph (i.e., 547 common strong edges, their weights, and their relationships). However, including weaker edges 548 (beyond the top 10%) led to a striking deterioration in the node-strength MMD. Because MMD 549 compares the distributions across subjects, this degradation reveals a profound mismatch at the 550 population level. Crucially, this severe decline was also observed among the empirical datasets 551 themselves (“Ref.” in Figure 6A). Rather than a generative failure, this phenomenon strongly reflects 552 the inherent biological properties of structural brain networks: in contrast to the top-strong edges, 553 weaker connections are known to exhibit stronger individual variability across subjects. 36 

554 Consequently, the heterogeneity of weak connections prevents the empirical data itself from 555 forming a stable, convergent distribution for the models to learn. 556 Conversely, as anticipated from the architectural bias discussed in Section 4.1.2, DDPM struggled 557 to pinpoint exact edge coordinates in the SC dataset. Qualitative observations reveal that DDPM 558 generated SC-like connection textures, but it cannot pinpoint exact coordinates or maintain 559 consistency across individual samples. This directly reflects the bias observed in the SBM dataset. 560 This lack of coordinate consistency prevents the formation of a stable anatomical backbone, 561 manifesting as a blurry mean adjacency matrix and high MMD scores across most metric-threshold 562 combinations (Figure 6A). These contrasting results underscore that strict coordinate fidelity is an 563 essential architectural requirement for faithfully reproducing the rigid neuroanatomical backbone 564 of empirical SC data. 565 

4.2.2. Anatomy -Assisted Approximation of SC Topology 566 

Beyond architectural biases, the models’ generative paradigms interacted uniquely with the 567 modality-specific properties of SC data. Reflecting its global distributional matching paradigm 568 discussed in Section 4.1.3, WGAN-GP demonstrated highly balanced generative performance, 569 ranking first on approximately 75% of the evaluated metrics. Notably, it achieved the best 570 performance in spectral and embedding MMDs across all threshold criteria for the SC dataset 571 (Figure 6A). For instance, when the most significant digerence was observed in the SC dataset (0.2 572 threshold), WGAN-GP achieved a spectral MMD of 0.00052 and an embedding MMD of 0.0064, both 573 more than ten times lower than those of other models. 574 Interestingly, while VAE and WGAN-GP struggled to replicate global topological metrics in synthetic 575 datasets, they demonstrated a remarkable capability to approximate these structures in empirical 576 SC data. Specifically, VAE and WGAN-GP—especially WGAN-GP—reproduced the distribution 577 peaks of modularity and small-worldness ( 𝜎 ) and their gradual shifts across varying sparsity 578 thresholds (Figure 6B, C). This discrepancy could be attributed to the inherent underlying rules of 579

Unlike purely mathematical graphs, SC networks are constrained by human brain 580 anatomy, formed through optimization between wiring costs and network egiciency. 37 Models can 581 capitalize on these probability biases of edge presence (e.g., highly probable anatomically 582 anchored long-range corrections). By learning these spatial probability biases, the models could 583 indirectly approximate modularity and small-worldness. Consequently, the success in the SC 584 dataset could be better interpreted as an anatomy-assisted approximation rather than genuine 585 topological learning. 586 

4.2.3. Implications for SC Data Augmentation 587 

Extrapolating these generative characteristics to potential downstream applications, WGAN-GP 588 emerges as the most promising baseline for future SC data augmentation tasks. Since data 589 augmentation aims to increase data diversity for better generalization, the over-smoothing 590 tendency of VAEs, biasing generation toward high-probability edges, could be a disadvantage for 591 general classification or regression. Similarly, DDPMs struggle to reliably preserve the exact sparse 592 coordinates of the anatomical network. Conversely, WGAN-GP captured overall network traits, 593 especially for strong edges, preserving graph variety. WGAN-GP outperforms VAE not only in 594 maintaining data diversity but also in accurately reproducing the strong major backbone of the brain, 595 as evidenced by MMD scores in the top 5% of the strongest connections (Figure 6A). Preserving 596 these major structural hubs is crucial, as neurological disorders, such as AD and frontotemporal 597 dementia, selectively target these hubs. 38 Data augmentation with models that egectively 598 reproduce main structures shows potential for studying skeletal abnormalities as biomarkers. Thus, 599 WGAN-GP is recommended for SC data augmentation, as it balances data diversity and topological 600 properties, consistent with its success on the synthetic dataset. However, its impact on 601 downstream tasks such as prediction or classification still needs further validation. 602 

# 4.3. Limitation s and Future Directions 603 

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the observed performance digerences may 604 depend on the specific implementations, architectures, and hyperparameters used in this study. In 605 particular, DDPM was implemented with a U-Net architecture, whereas VAE and WGAN-GP were 606 implemented with MLP-based architectures. Therefore, the egects of the generative paradigm and 607 network architecture cannot be fully separated. Furthermore, our decision to omit data scaling 608 might have impacted the training stability and optimal performance of the generative models. 609 Future studies should evaluate alternative architectures, including MLP-based digusion models or 610 graph-specific architectures, to determine whether the observed tendencies generalize beyond the 611 present implementation. 612 Second, this study focused on the structural fidelity of generated graphs rather than directly 613 evaluating their impact on downstream prediction or classification tasks. Although the present 614 results suggest that WGAN-GP provides a balanced baseline for preserving major SC structures, its 615 practical egectiveness as a data augmentation method should be validated in task-specific settings. 616 

# 5. Conclusion 617 

In this study, we evaluated three representative deep generative models—VAE, WGAN-GP, and 618 DDPM—to elucidate their model-specific generative biases and capabilities in capturing the 619 topological features of SC data. By combining synthetic graph datasets with controlled 620
