## Multiscale Dynamic Dependence Estimation over Networks

Abstract 

In numerous scientific and industrial settings, observed multivariate time series are often non-stationary in nature, i.e., comprise data whose second order properties vary over time. An addi-tional feature of many modern datasets is that the cross-dependencies of such series are structured by an underlying network, giving rise to complex interactions between temporal dynamics and network topology. In this article we propose Locally Stationary Wavelet processes on Networks (Net-LSW), a new framework for modelling multiscale, time-varying dependencies that explicitly incorporates the network structure. Unlike traditional multivariate approaches, the Net-LSW pro-cess encodes the graph directly in the covariance structure of the process’s random increments. We also introduce the concept of the local partial correlation graph , which connects edges in the graph to non-zero entries in the time- and scale- dependent dependence structure of a nonsta-tionary process. For inference on the local cross-nodal (partial) dependence, we develop a novel subprocess-based estimation scheme and establish its desirable consistency properties. Simulation studies further demonstrate that the proposed framework accurately recovers evolving dependence structures whilst respecting the underlying graph topology. Finally, we apply our framework to daily stock price volatilities across a global bank network, demonstrating its ability to capture multiscale, highly nonstationary dependencies and identify time-varying systemic shifts during major financial shocks, including Brexit and the COVID-19 pandemic.

1 Introduction 

Many modern datasets comprise multivariate time series whose cross-dependencies are structured by an underlying, known network topology. In such settings, the information recorded at each node is often a (second order) nonstationary process, giving rise to complex interactions between time-evolving temporal dynamics and the network configuration. Examples of this rich paradigm span a wide array of domains, including structural connections across localised regions in brain networks (Achard and Bullmore, 2007; Fiecas and Ombao, 2011), geographical links in climate monitoring topologies, and multi-channel transaction streams in financial systems (Demirer et al., 2018). Because these systems change dynamically over time, modelling them requires robust statistical frameworks capable of capturing the intricate interactions between time-varying temporal mechanics, multi-scale structural behaviours, and the network’s spatial constraints. To model time-varying systems with evolving spectral properties, the literature on nonstation-ary time series has traditionally relied on the foundational concept of evolutionary spectra intro-duced by Priestley (1965). This paradigm was subsequently revolutionised by Dahlhaus (1997) (see also Dahlhaus (2012)) through the mathematical formalisation of locally stationary processes , which model time series whose statistical properties change smoothly over time, allowing them to be approx-imated locally by stationary processes. While these classical models operate in the Fourier domain, wavelet-based models have emerged as an invaluable tool for extracting highly localised features si-multaneously across both time and frequency. The foundation for time-varying spectrum modelling using wavelets is the Locally Stationary Wavelet (LSW) process introduced by Nason et al. (2000) This univariate formulation was extended to model localised linear dependencies between pairs of nonstationary series by Sanderson et al. (2010), and later generalised to larger, unconstrained systems via the Multivariate Locally Stationary Wavelet (MvLSW) framework by Park et al. (2014). Recently, a research direction in the statistical literature has been focussed on modelling multi-variate time series structured by a known graph topology. In particular, parsimonious time-domain models—most notably Generalised Network Autoregressive (GNAR) processes (Knight et al., 2016, 2020)—regularise high-dimensional interactions via node neighbourhood parametrisations, a paradigm recently extended to accommodate time-varying parameters and latent groupings (Chen et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026). However, models for nonstationary time series which explicitly capturing time-varying, scale-dependent cross dependencies while honouring the underlying graph topology are lacking. To bridge this gap, this article introduces Locally Stationary Wavelet processes on Networks ( Net-LSW ), a new framework for modelling multi-scale, time-varying dependencies that explicitly incor-porates the network structure. Unlike traditional unconstrained multivariate approaches, the Net-LSW process restricts the node-specific amplitudes to a diagonal transfer function matrix and encodes the graph architecture into the covariance structure of the process’s random innovation increments. By embedding the network topology into these internal innovation mechanics, our framework perfectly aligns the time-varying, scale-dependent spectral properties of the system with the physical network architecture. A primary theoretical contribution of this article is the formal connection between these network-structured innovations and the foundational time-series graphical modelling literature established by Dahlhaus (2000a). By shifting from the classical stationary Fourier paradigm to our localised wavelet domain model, we introduce a generalised notion of a local partial correlation graph . This multi-scale network formulation complements recent process-wide conditional specifications designed for stationary series (Bhattacharya et al., 2025), as well as infinite-dimensional operator representations used to study nonstationary graph structures (Basu and Rao, 2023). Crucially, we establish a funda-mental structural equivalence under the Net-LSW framework: the absence of an edge between nodes 2in the underlying physical network exactly corresponds to a structural zero entry in the associated localised network precision matrix across all wavelet scales and rescaled time points. This direct mathematical alignment allows the inverse evolutionary wavelet spectral domain to be uniquely inter-preted as the time-varying reflection of the graph architecture. To estimate the local cross-nodal dependence quantities under the proposed model, our approach differs fundamentally from both traditional time series graphical models and conventional multivari-ate wavelet estimation. In the classical time and Fourier domains, tracking nonstationary conditional dependencies requires inverting infinite-dimensional covariance operators (e.g., Basu and Rao, 2023; Krampe and Rao, 2024), a task that is intractable in practice. Conversely, traditional estimation meth-ods for (unconstrained) nonstationary wavelet models apply a joint linear matrix correction across all scales simultaneously (Park et al., 2014). However, this multi-scale mixing contaminates the net-work’s topology constraints, meaning that enforced structural zeros in the raw parameters do not map to structural zeros in the final inverse evolutionary spectrum. Drawing intuition from Wu et al. (2026), who introduced a subprocess-based approach in the context of multi-scale wavelet coherence estima-tion, we resolve this topological misalignment by developing a network-constrained scale-by-scale estimation procedure. This approach enables us to formulate a finite-dimensional, convex, network-penalised optimisation problem for each individual scale. We establish the asymptotic mean-squared consistency of our local smoothed subprocess spectral estimators and prove that optimisation via graphical covariance selection (Hastie et al., 2009) yields unique, consistent network topology estima-tors. Finally, since latent subprocesses are unobservable in practice, we introduce a robust projection method to reconstruct them from the aggregate observed coefficients, proving that (asymptotically) their underlying covariance structures match. We illustrate the utility of our proposed framework by analysing the time-scale dependency struc-tures of daily stock price volatilities across a high-dimensional network of major international bank-ing institutions. In this context, financial networks are conventionally defined by computing direc-tional spillovers via generalised forecast error variance decompositions (GFEVD) in the time domain (Diebold and Yilmaz, 2009; Demirer et al., 2018). Using our framework with a GFEVD connected-ness graph, we show that direct, cross-bank dependencies shift dynamically across both time windows and wavelet scales, capturing clear structural changes during the Brexit referendum, the COVID-19 pandemic, and global macro-financial shocks. The remainder of this article is organised as follows. In Section 2, we establish our network notation and provide a review of the necessary LSW and MvLSW background material. Section 3 details the formal mathematical formulation of the novel Net-LSW process and its essential properties. In Section 4, we propose the wavelet-domain Local Partial Correlation Graph and prove the structural equivalence between missing network edges and zeros in the localised precision matrix. Section 5 presents our subprocess-based inference framework and establishes its asymptotic consistency theory. Section 6 evaluates finite-sample performance via controlled numerical simulations, and Section 7 demonstrates an application of the framework to real-world data. All technical proofs and auxiliary derivations are deferred to the Appendix.

24 6.4 Effect of Network Sparsity 

In this section, we study how varying levels of edge density in the underlying network affect estimation stability. We isolate this structural effect by fixing the dimension of the network to N = 25 nodes (as exemplified in Figure 2, right panel) and varying the underlying edge generation probability ℘ ∈{10% , 30% , 50% }. To achieve this, the underlying topologies are generated as Erd¨ os-Renyi random graphs using the igraph package in R.The experimental design for this simulation is organised as follows: 1. For each sparsity level ℘, we randomly generate K = 30 independent Erd¨ os-Renyi network topologies. 2. For each network, we simulate R = 50 independent time-series replicates according to the data generating process detailed in Section 6.1. 3. For each of the 30 network graphs, we compute its MSE and Bias 2 averaged over its R = 50 

runs. The impact of network sparsity is studied across the same series of scenarios (Scenarios 1, 2, and 3) and sample lengths ( T ∈ { 512 , 1024 , 2048 }) used in the previous section. To visually analyse the distributional performance and sensitivity under changing edge configurations, the results across the 30 random graphs for Scenario 2 are presented as boxplots in Figures 3 and 4. For completeness, the corresponding boxplots for Scenarios 1 and 3 are presented in Appendix D. For both the squared bias ( Bias 2) and mean squared error ( MSE ), the proposed Net-LSW es-timator (red) consistently achieves lower median errors and narrower interquartile ranges than the network-agnostic MvLSW of Park et al. (2014) (purple) across both scales 1 and 4, tracking the true subprocess benchmark (blue). As the sample size T increases from 512 to 2048, both the proposed estimator and the blue benchmark exhibit a clear downward shift in their error distributions alongside a visible tightening of the boxplots, highlighting empirical consistency. Conversely, the network-agnostic MvLSW fails to show a clear systematic error reduction and maintains wide distributional spreads across sample sizes. Crucially, as the network density increases from ℘ = 10% to ℘ = 50% ,although the overall error baseline shifts slightly upward for all cases, our proposed estimator retains its performance and tracking stability over the network-agnostic alternative, confirming its robustness even under denser network topologies.

7.3 Localised Time-Scale Dependence Structures 

To evaluate how the nonstationary time series behave across this banking network, we focus on se-lected institutional pairs that capture distinct systemic, regional, and cross-border transmission mech-anisms. We contrast network-informed coherence and network-informed partial coherence to distin-guish direct dependence channels from broader, market-wide comovement. Throughout this analysis, we plot the absolute values of the respective (partial) coherence metrics. 

The COVID-19 Shock and US Money-Center Banks 

We first examine the dependence dynamics between JPMorgan Chase and Citigroup , two central nodes in global dollar funding markets. Figure 6 shows the estimated dependence across scales around the COVID-19 shock period. Both the coherence and its version that conditions on the information in the other nodes, the partial coherence, reveal the similar dynamic features and scales, albeit of different intensities. There is a notable sharply localised feature around the early 2020 period, concentrated at high-to-medium frequencies ( j = 1 to 7). The strongest conditioned effect occurs at the finest scale (j = 1 ), where partial coherence reaches its peak magnitude for this pair. Disconnected Pairs and Constraint Illustration 

To show how the network constraints operate, we examine the pairs HSBC–Barclays and JPMorgan Chase–Deutsche Bank . These institutions share no direct edge in the fixed network topology in Figure 5. Figure 9 reports their coherence estimates. The local coherence displays bouts of strong uncondi-tional co-movement driven by shared global factors, punctuated by local- and world-events. Because no direct edge connects these pairs in the underlying graph, the framework sets their partial coherence uniformly to zero. This demonstrates how the model filters out common market factors and enforces the network structure. 

8 Discussion 

In this article, we have addressed the challenge of capturing multi-scale, time-varying linear cross-dependencies in nonstationary multivariate time series, structured by an underlying network topology. While traditional frameworks allow for (unconstrained) dependence estimation, they are network-agnostic and computationally expensive—estimating all pairwise dependencies as if the system were fully connected—thus leading to severe over-parametrization in high dimensions. In contrast, our Locally Stationary Wavelet process on Networks (Net-LSW) framework accommodates the network-induced topological constraints by encoding the existing edge connections directly into the formula-tion of our proposed model. 31 Network Coherence: HSBC <−−> Barclays

Note the Brexit impact (left) and well-aligned COVID-19 market effects of different intensities. The theoretical contributions of this work are twofold. Firstly, we propose a local partial correla-tion graph which is able to characterise the graphical structure underpinning the observed nonstation-ary signal in the wavelet domain, in a way that mirrors the classical Fourier partial correlation graphs of Dahlhaus (2000a) in the stationary setting. Specifically, the absence of a physical edge corresponds exactly to a structural zero in the network time-localised precision matrix Θj (z) across all scales j

and rescaled times z. Secondly, we develop a computationally tractable scale-by-scale subprocess-based estimation procedure. By projecting the observed signal to (approximate) latent subprocesses, we are able to form a time-localised, multi-scale, convex network-penalised objective function. We establish that optimizing this objective via graphical covariance selection yields unique, mean-squared consistent estimators, extending classical estimation schemes to the locally stationary wavelet setting. The consistency properties of our estimators are strongly validated by our simulation studies. In our modelling framework, the network topology is known and fixed across time. A direction for future research would relax the static network assumption, to facilitate time-varying edge inclusion, which would pose theoretical and computational challenges, e.g., requiring complex penalisation.

Figures 14 through 17 display the empirical tracking accuracy metrics under the full multiple-scale setting. Under the multiple-scale environment of Scenario 3, the performance gap widens significantly across scales j = 1 through 4, where both the true and approximate subprocess estimators maintain much lower medians and tighter error bounds than the standard MvLSW framework. This distinction is particularly pronounced in the partial coherence metrics, where the MvLSW boxplots remain highly elevated on the vertical axis across both ℘ = 10% and ℘ = 30% . However, at the coarsest scale (j = 5 ), the standard MvLSW framework produces lower MSE values compared to the subprocess estimators. Crucially, as reported in Figure 15, MvLSW displays a very high squared bias at this same scale. Because its MSE remains low despite this large bias, it implies that the MvLSW estimator operates with an artificially low variance at scale 5, tightly tracking an incorrect signal trajectory. As a specific example, Figure 18 displays the partial coherence trajectory averaged across the simulations for a representative node pair. Here, it is evident that the MvLSW framework fails to accurately capture the true underlying energy profile at this scale, flatlining along an incorrect path despite its low variance.
