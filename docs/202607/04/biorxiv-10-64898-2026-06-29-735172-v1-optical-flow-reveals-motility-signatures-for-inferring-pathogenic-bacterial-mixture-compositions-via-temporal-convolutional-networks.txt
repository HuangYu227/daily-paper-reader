# Abstract 

With the rapid expansion of global food demand, aquaculture has become a critical pillar for future food security. However, aquaculture systems remain highly vulnerable to pathogenic bacteria, and rapid identification of antagonistic microbes is essential for sustainable disease control. Conventional evaluation approaches rely on fluorescence labeling or post-culture assays, limiting the ability to quantify dynamic interactions in mixed microbial populations in a real-time and label-free manner. Here, we propose a computational framework for classifying the mixing ratio of Vibrio harveyi and environmental bacteria using time-series motion features extracted from microscopy videos. We defined 24 interpretable motility descriptors and employed a Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN) to learn their temporal structure. The proposed method achieved a classification accuracy of 93.3%, outperforming conventional static statistical approaches and alternative machine learning models. These findings indicate that mixture discrimination in microbial communities is governed not by absolute motility magnitude, but by collective alignment and its temporal stability. Our study establishes a time-resolved computational framework for quantifying dynamic collective order in mixed microbial populations and highlights its potential for label-free automated screening and robotic microbiological applications.

# Introduction 1

In recent years, global food demand has expanded rapidly due to population growth and changes 2

in dietary patterns. According to estimates by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), the 3

world population is projected to reach approximately 9.3 billion by 2050, and food production 4

will need to increase by nearly 60% to meet this demand [1]. Under these circumstances, 5

aquaculture has emerged as a critical pillar for ensuring future food security. However, 6

aquaculture systems are highly vulnerable to infections caused by pathogenic bacteria, and 7

climate change—particularly rising sea temperatures—further increases the risk of emerging 8

pathogens. To ensure the development of sustainable aquaculture systems, rapid pathogen 9

detection methodologies must be established. While traditional diagnostic 10 

frameworks—including microbial culture and molecular techniques such as PCR—remain the 11 

gold standard, they are frequently constrained by significant time delays, high operational costs, 12 

and a heavy reliance on specialized laboratory infrastructure [2]. 13 

Cell identification based on imaging has been widely explored because it offers a simple and 14 

accessible approach that does not require complex molecular biological procedures or specialized 15 

analytical instruments. Such image-based methods enable rapid and non-invasive analysis, 16 

making them attractive for a variety of biological and clinical applications [3]. In recent years, 17 

there has been a notable increase in studies employing machine learning techniques to improve 18 

the accuracy and automation of cell identification, leveraging advances in computational power 19 

and data-driven modeling [4]. However, compared to eukaryotic cells, bacteria are significantly 20 

smaller, posing a fundamental challenge for image-based identification. Under conventional 21 

optical microscopy, bacterial cells often lack sufficiently distinct morphological features, making 22 

reliable classification from static images difficult [5 –7]. As a result, image-based approaches for 23 

bacterial identification remain limited in their performance, highlighting the need for alternative 24 

strategies or additional sources of information beyond static imaging. 25 

Vibrio harveyi is a widely distributed marine pathogen characterized by high flagellar 26 

motility and collective gene regulation via quorum sensing, making its rapid identification 27 

crucial for sustainable disease management in aquaculture [8]. Therefore, we hypothesized that 28 

analyzing time-lapse microscopy videos of microbial motility—rather than static images—would 29 

enable the identification of V. harveyi from diverse environmental bacterial populations without 30 

the need for fluorescent labeling or culture-based cloning. 31 

Conventional analyses of single-cell motility predominantly rely on human-defined statistical 32 

descriptors, such as mean velocity, mean squared displacement, directional distributions, and 33 

velocity histograms [9, 10]. While these hand-crafted metrics provide useful summaries of 34 

fundamental motion characteristics [11], they inherently compress temporal information by 35 

averaging or aggregating measurements over time. As a consequence, sustained changes in 36 

motion and the sequential context of trajectories are not explicitly preserved, leading to the loss 37 

of dynamic structural information [12]. In heterogeneous microbial populations, this limitation 38 

becomes particularly critical, as the temporal dependencies and continuous evolution of motion 39 

provide essential information reflecting underlying behavioral differences. Static, pre-defined 40 

descriptors are therefore insufficient to fully capture these complex dynamics. In contrast, 41 

leveraging automated motion feature extraction allows for the direct modeling of temporal 42 

sequences, enabling the spontaneous discovery of hidden, non-intuitive motility patterns that 43 

human observers might overlook, and utilizing them as robust metrics for classification. 44 

Furthermore, deep learning methods have recently demonstrated strong performance in 45 

image and time-series analysis [13, 14]; however, their application to microbial motility analysis 46 

remains limited. [15, 16] Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are well-suited for extracting 47 

spatial features [14] but are not inherently designed to model long-range temporal dependencies. 48

Recurrent neural networks (RNNs), although theoretically capable of handling sequential data, 49 

are known to suffer from gradient instability and difficulty in learning long-term 50 

dependencies [17]. Furthermore, computational frameworks that explicitly represent motility 51 

descriptors as structured time-series inputs and learn their temporal dynamics in mixed microbial 52 

systems have not been sufficiently established. Therefore, there is a clear need for computational 53 

approaches that preserve and learn the temporal structure of motility features, enabling 54 

identification of dynamically evolving collective order in mixed microbial populations. 55 

In this study, we propose a computational framework for analyzing motility dynamics in 56 

mixed microbial populations while preserving temporal structure. Specifically, we extract 57 

motion features of cell populations from time-series microscopy images using optical flow. This 58 

representation captures velocity fields and their spatiotemporal variations, including directional 59 

tendencies, spatial organization, and temporal fluctuations of collective motion. By transforming 60 

raw image sequences into a motion-based representation, our approach enables analysis within a 61 

physically interpretable feature space, facilitating the interpretation of learned representations in 62 

terms of biologically meaningful motility characteristics. We then employ a Temporal 63 

Convolutional Network (TCN) [18] to learn temporal patterns in the extracted motion features. 64 

TCN [18] leverages dilated causal convolutions, enabling efficient modeling of long-range 65 

temporal dependencies while maintaining stable training dynamics. By operating on motion 66 

representations derived from optical flow, the model captures subtle temporal signatures of 67 

bacterial motility that are not readily discernible from individual frames or by human 68 

observation. As a result, our approach enables the estimation of the relative abundance of V. 69 

harveyi within heterogeneous bacterial populations from microscopy image sequences. 70 

Although the present study focuses on estimating the abundance of V. harveyi in mixed 71 

populations, the proposed framework is generalizable and has the potential to be extended to the 72 

detection and characterization of other bacterial species. 73 

# Methods 74 

## Dataset 75 

In this study, we analyzed images of bacterial samples consisting of the marine pathogen V. 76 

harveyi and environmental bacteria. V. harveyi was provided by Prof. K. Koiwai [19]. 77 

Environmental bacteria were collected from soil sampled from a flower bed at the Korakuen 78 

Campus of Chuo University (Bunkyo, Tokyo, Japan). The bacteria were isolated through 79 

dilution, centrifugation, and filtration. Both bacterial strains were stored as glycerol stocks in a 80 

freezer. For experimental use, a small aliquot was inoculated into a culture medium (2.5% Brain 81 

Heart Infusion medium; Becton & Dickinson, Cat#211065, supplemented with 1.5% NaCl) and 82 

incubated overnight with shaking at 25 ◦C. The cell concentrations of both bacterial populations 83 

were then estimated from microscopy images and diluted to the desired concentration 84 

(5 × 10 8 cells/mL). The mixing ratio between V. harveyi and environmental bacteria was 85 

systematically varied from 0:10 to 10:0 in increments of 0.5, resulting in 21 distinct mixture 86 

conditions. For each mixture ratio, independent mixing and imaging experiments were 87 

conducted. 88 

Microscopic observations were performed using an inverted microscope (Nikon Ti [20]). 89 

Time-lapse images were recorded with a CCD camera under the bright-field conditions (Fig 1). 90 

Each video was captured at a spatial resolution of 1920 × 1200 pixels. For each sample, 27 91 

consecutive frames were acquired at a frame rate of 9 fps. For each mixture condition, imaging 92 

was repeated 10 times while varying the field of view in order to account for spatial 93 

heterogeneity and diverse distribution patterns of bacterial populations. To reduce spatial bias 94 

and increase the number of independent training samples, each 1920 × 1200 frame was 95 

subdivided into six non-overlapping regions of 640 × 600 pixels. This preprocessing step 96 

generated six sub-sequences from each original images. Across all mixture conditions, a total of 97

## Motion Feature Extraction 104 

In the following, we describe the proposed motion feature extraction framework, illustrating each 105 

step with concrete examples from the experimental data. Pixel-wise motion between consecutive 106 

frames was estimated using the dense optical flow method proposed by Farneb¨ ack [21]. This 107 

approach approximates local image neighborhoods with quadratic polynomials and estimates 108 

displacement by analyzing changes in polynomial coefficients across frames, enabling dense 109 

estimation of subtle and continuous motion. In microscopy videos, both individual-level 110 

micro-movements and population-level collective flows can coexist; thus, obtaining dense 111 

motion information at the pixel level is essential. Fig 1 shows snapshots from the original 112 

consecutive images at three different mixing ratios of V. harveyi and environmental bacteria. 113 

The bacteria appear as black dots or rods, making it virtually impossible to identify their species 114 

from still images alone. From the estimated flow field (𝑢, 𝑣 ), we extracted motility descriptors to 115 

represent collective dynamics in a low-dimensional yet interpretable manner. In this study, the 116 

features were grouped into the following six categories: (i) velocity statistics, (ii) directional 117 

statistics, (iii) motion diversity indices, (iv) frequency-domain features, (v) spatial gradient and 118 

autocorrelation indices, and (vi) local motion pattern indices. These categories were designed to 119 

quantify collective motion from complementary perspectives, including motion intensity, 120 

directional order, distributional complexity, temporal fluctuation structure, spatial continuity, and 121 

local deformation patterns. The 24 motility features used in this study were designed to 122 

systematically and comprehensively characterize the motion state of bacterial populations from a 123 

physical perspective. The above six categories—covering velocity, direction, temporal variation, 124 

and spatial order—were organized by referring to physically meaningful quantities commonly 125 

used in active matter research and collective motion analysis (e.g., order parameters and 126 

alignment statistics) [22–24], as well as standard frequency-domain and statistical measures 127 

widely used in signal processing [25]. Therefore, the proposed feature space is not an empirical 128 

collection of arbitrary statistics but a structured representation grounded in theoretical 129 

frameworks of collective motion. 130

## Temporal Convolutional Network 256 

To classify bacterial mixture ratios from the extracted motion feature sequences, we employed a 257 

TCN [18]. TCN models temporal dependencies using one-dimensional convolutions and can 258 

efficiently capture long-range temporal relationships through dilated convolutions [18, 26]. 259 

Compared with RNNs, TCNs allow parallel computation and are less susceptible to gradient 260 

vanishing or exploding problems. Fig 9 illustrates the overall processing flow of the proposed 261 

TCN-based classification workflow. The input to the network is a time series of 24-dimensional 262 

motion features. Each sample consists of 27 frames, and the input sequence is represented as 263 

𝑋 = {𝑥 1, 𝑥 2, . . . , 𝑥 𝑇 }, 𝑥 𝑡 ∈ R24 , 𝑇 = 27 . (22) The first layer of the network is a 1 × 1 convolutional projection layer that maps the 24 input 264 

features to a 128-dimensional channel space. This layer learns linear combinations of the motion 265 

descriptors while providing a higher-dimensional representation suitable for subsequent 266

## Feature Importance Analysis 319 

To analyze which motion features contributed most to the classification of bacterial mixture 320 

ratios, we conducted a feature importance analysis using permutation importance. Permutation 321 

importance evaluates the contribution of each feature by measuring the decrease in model 322 

performance when the relationship between that feature and the target variable is disrupted. In 323 

this study, the importance of each motion feature was evaluated using the trained TCN [18]. 324 

Specifically, the motion feature sequences consisted of 24 feature channels extracted from 325 

optical flow fields. For each feature channel, the temporal order of the feature values within each 326 

sample was randomly shuffled while keeping all other features unchanged. This operation 327 

destroys the temporal structure of the selected feature while preserving its statistical distribution. 328 

The permuted feature sequences were then input into the trained TCN [18] model, and the 329 

classification accuracy was recalculated using the test dataset. The importance score of each 330 

feature was defined as the decrease in classification accuracy caused by the permutation. To 331 

reduce the influence of randomness, the permutation procedure was repeated multiple times for 332 

each feature channel, and the mean and standard deviation of the accuracy drop were computed. 333 

In this study, five repetitions were performed for each feature. Features that produced larger 334 

decreases in classification accuracy were interpreted as having greater contributions to the 335 

classification of bacterial mixture ratios. The resulting permutation importance values provide 336 

insights into which physical aspects of bacterial collective motion are most informative for 337 

mixture-ratio estimation. 338 

# Results 339 

## Overall Classification Performance 340 

To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, the extracted 24 motion features were 341 

input to TCN [18] to classify the mixture ratios of V. harveyi and environmental bacteria. The 342 

resulting confusion matrix is shown in Fig 10. The proposed model achieved an overall 343 

classification accuracy of 93.3%, demonstrating that the mixture ratios can be reliably identified 344 

from the motion features. 345 

To further analyze the performance across individual classes, precision, recall, and F1 score 346 

were calculated for each mixture condition. These metrics are defined as follows. Precision is 347 

defined as the ratio of true positives to all samples predicted as positive for a given class, 348 

expressed as Precision = TP /( TP + FP ), where TP denotes true positives and FP denotes false 349 

positives. A high precision value indicates that when the model predicts a sample as belonging 350 

to a particular mixture-ratio class, this prediction is likely to be correct, meaning the model 351 

generates few false alarms. Recall is defined as the ratio of true positives to all samples that 352 

actually belong to a given class, expressed as Recall = TP /( TP + FN ), where FN denotes false 353 

negatives. A high recall value indicates that the model successfully identifies the majority of 354 

samples belonging to a particular mixture-ratio class, meaning few true instances are missed. 355 

The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, defined as 356 

F1 = 2 · ( Precision × Recall )/( Precision + Recall ). This metric provides a balanced evaluation 357 

that accounts for both false positives and false negatives, and is particularly informative when 358 

the performance trade-off between precision and recall needs to be summarized in a single value. 359

To further investigate the relationship between motion features and mixture ratios, a 453

provides more stable and consistent discrimination, suggesting that classification is more 461 

suitable for mixture-ratio analysis in this study. Nevertheless, the regression results confirm that 462 

motion features contain continuous information about population composition, supporting the 463 

validity of the proposed feature representation. 464 

# Discussion & Conclusion 465 

In this study, we demonstrated that a TCN based on optical flow can accurately classify the 466 

abundance ratio of mixed bacterial populations, a task that is virtually impossible using static 467 

bright-field images. The combination of optical flow and TCN proved highly suitable for this 468 

approach. Optical flow captures dense, pixel-level spatiotemporal variations in velocity fields 469 

without the heavy computational burden of tracking individual cells, while the TCN effectively 470 

learns the long-range temporal dependencies and hierarchical structures inherent in these 471 

continuous motion features. 472

Permutation importance analysis revealed that the “collectiveness” of motion, specifically 473 

directional concentration, made the highest contribution to the classification performance. It is 474 

widely known in the field of active matter physics that bacterial populations can give rise to 475 

emergent collective behaviors—such as swarms, vortices, and active turbulence—driven by 476 

hydrodynamic interactions and steric collisions, particularly at high cell densities or near phase 477 

interfaces. Although careful verification is required to determine the exact physical relationship 478 

between our classification metrics and the well-documented bacterial collective motion, it is 479 

highly intriguing that our model identified macroscopic collective alignment as a key 480 

discriminator. Furthermore, features related to temporal fluctuations in bacterial motion were 481 

ranked among the most important contributors. This result likely reflects the highly motile and 482 

temporally variable swimming behavior characteristic of Vibrio species. In this pilot study, we 483 

validated the classification of specific pathogenic bacteria against a heterogeneous and undefined 484 

mixture of environmental bacteria. To clarify the biological significance of these motion metrics 485 

and their direct relationship to actual bacterial kinematics, systematic investigations using 486 

defined bacterial mixtures with known motility profiles are necessary. This remains a fascinating 487 

theme for future research. 488 

Regardless, the present workflow, which can classify the characteristics of bacterial 489 

populations directly from bright-field time-lapse images, holds great promise for various 490 

applications. Beyond the simple detection of specific microorganisms, such as pathogens, this 491 

approach could be applied to evaluate drug responses and antibiotic resistance using motility 492 

indicators, or to screen bacteria using microcompartments, such as microchambers or droplets. 493 

Because this approach relies on standard bright-field microscopy and imposes a much lower 494 

computational burden than single-cell tracking, it demonstrates significant potential, especially 495 

in low-resource environments, as it circumvents the need for expensive analytical equipment or 496 

complex molecular techniques such as fluorescent labeling or genetic manipulation. Moreover, 497 

this framework is not limited to swimming motility; it can handle any biological features that 498 

exhibit temporal changes. For example, it could be applied to monitor critical microbial 499 

indicators, such as cell proliferation dynamics or the degradation of microscopic substrates (e.g., 500 

cellulose), significantly broadening its utility. 501 

As this study serves as a pilot validation of the combination of optical flow and TCN, 502 

continuous and detailed verification is required to determine its universal applicability for 503 

identifying or classifying a broader range of microorganisms or their communities. In particular, 504 

the effects of cell density, imaging conditions, time-lapse intervals, and frame rates on the 505 

classification accuracy must be rigorously evaluated in future studies. Nevertheless, because 506 

optical flow can capture a diverse array of kinematic and temporal features, it is highly likely to 507 

accurately capture subtle, non-obvious dynamic signatures that might be missed by human 508 

observers or by conventional methods that rely on predefined, static evaluation axes. This study 509 

represents the first attempt to classify using machine learning the temporal dynamics of 510 

microbial population images, and we anticipate significant developments and broad applications 511 

based on this label-free, time-resolved framework in the future. 512
